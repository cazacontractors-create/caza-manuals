# Caza Contractors — Flooring Estimating Manual

**Purpose:** A trade-expert reference for the AI estimator covering floor finishes — tile (ceramic/porcelain/stone), hardwood & engineered wood, resilient (LVP/LVT/sheet vinyl/laminate), and carpet. Same structure as the build-trade manuals: for each system it defines the labor tasks that drive time, the factors that move them, the material list with units/coverage, special equipment, the dimensions required for a quote, and the best-practices that change material or labor.

**Flooring estimates on area (SF) — but the substrate and prep are where jobs are won or lost.** The finish material is the visible part; the **subfloor prep, leveling, moisture control, and underlayment** determine whether the floor lasts or fails. The same room is a very different job depending on what's under the new floor and what condition it's in.

**⚠️ SUBSTRATE / MOISTURE / WATERPROOFING BOUNDARY:** Flooring failures are usually *substrate* failures — an out-of-flat subfloor (tile cracks, laminate gaps), trapped moisture (cupping wood, adhesive failure, mold), or missing waterproofing (leaking tile showers/wet areas). Throughout, **⚠️** marks where the *substrate, moisture, or waterproofing* decision matters beyond laying the surface — these are where to verify conditions, not eyeball them. (Tile shower/wet-area waterproofing also cross-refs the Plumbing Manual — assign ownership clearly.)

**Climate / building context (this market):** Cold-climate concerns touch flooring — slab moisture (cross-ref Concrete vapor barrier), basement/below-grade moisture (favor moisture-tolerant materials there ⚠️), wood movement with seasonal humidity swings (acclimation + expansion gaps ⚠️), and radiant-floor heat compatibility (cross-ref HVAC — not all flooring suits radiant ⚠️). Mudrooms/entries take wet/snowy/salty abuse (durable, water-tolerant materials).

**How to use this manual (for the AI):**
1. Identify the flooring type(s) + the **substrate** (what's under it) + its condition ⚠️.
2. Use **Required Dimensions**; estimate conservatively + state assumptions.
3. Build material from **area ÷ coverage × (1+waste)** + underlayment/prep + trim/transitions.
4. Estimate labor from **Labor Tasks** (MH/unit), apply **Modifiers** (one combined factor, capped).
5. Add **Equipment**; apply **Best-Practice Notes** (incl. substrate/moisture ⚠️).

**Units key:** SF = square foot · LF = linear foot (trim/transitions) · EA = each · subfloor/substrate = what the flooring goes over · underlayment = the layer between subfloor + finish · MH = man-hours · ⚠️ = substrate/moisture/waterproofing — verify conditions

**Waste guidance:** Most flooring: **~10% waste** (cuts, defects, pattern); more for diagonal/herringbone/large-format/patterned (~15%+), complex room shapes, or natural stone (variation). Always order extra for attic stock + future repair.

# 1. TILE (CERAMIC / PORCELAIN / STONE)

## 1.1 System overview & types

Tile is individual units set in thin-set mortar over a prepared, flat, often-waterproofed substrate, with grouted joints. The most prep-and-skill-intensive flooring. The estimate is driven by **area (SF), tile size/type (large-format and stone are harder ⚠️), the substrate prep (flatness ⚠️ — tile is unforgiving of an out-of-flat subfloor), the underlayment (backer board / uncoupling membrane / waterproofing ⚠️), the pattern (diagonal/herringbone = waste + labor), and grouting** — plus wet-area waterproofing ⚠️.

**Types:**
- **Ceramic** — clay, glazed; economical, softer, wide range. Floors + walls.
- **Porcelain** — denser, harder, less porous (water-resistant), durable — the common quality floor tile; large-format common. Harder to cut.
- **Natural stone (marble/granite/travertine/slate)** ⚠️ — premium, variable (sorting), porous (needs sealing ⚠️), heavier, more skill/waste. Some need special handling (soft/etch-prone ⚠️).
- **Large-format tile** ⚠️ — big tiles (less grout, modern look) but demand a **very flat substrate** ⚠️ (lippage shows) + special handling/setting (back-butter, leveling clips).
- **Mosaic / small tile** — sheets; more grout, good for showers/floors needing slope/grip.
- **Glass / specialty** — accents.

## 1.2 Required dimensions for an accurate quote

- **★ Area (SF)** — floor area; + waste (more for pattern/large-format/stone).
- **★ Tile size + type** — large-format (flatness ⚠️) / stone (sealing ⚠️) / standard / mosaic.
- **★ Substrate + condition** ⚠️ — wood subfloor (needs backer/membrane + stiffness ⚠️), concrete slab (flatness/moisture ⚠️), existing floor (over or remove?). **Flatness is critical** ⚠️.
- **★ Underlayment/system** ⚠️ — cement backer board, uncoupling membrane (crack isolation), or waterproofing membrane (wet areas ⚠️).
- **★ Wet area?** ⚠️ — bathroom floor / shower (waterproofing + slope ⚠️ — cross-ref Plumbing).
- **★ Pattern + layout** — straight / diagonal / herringbone / brick (waste + labor); layout origin.
- **★ Grout** — type (cement/epoxy), joint width, color.
- **★ Subfloor stiffness (wood)** ⚠️ — tile needs a stiff floor (deflection cracks tile/grout ⚠️ — cross-ref Framing).
- **Transitions / thresholds / trim** — to other floors, edges.
- **Heated floor (radiant)?** ⚠️ — under-tile radiant (cross-ref HVAC).

## 1.7 Best-practice notes that change material or labor

1. **⚠️ Substrate flatness is everything — tile is unforgiving.** Tile (especially large-format ⚠️) needs a flat substrate (within tolerance) — an out-of-flat subfloor causes lippage (uneven edges), cracked tile/grout, and a bad job. **Checking + correcting flatness (self-leveler or grinding) is real, primary prep** ⚠️ — and the #1 tile best-practice. A bid that ignores substrate flatness is a red flag. Large-format demands the flattest substrate.
2. **⚠️ Wood subfloors need a rigid, stable base (backer/membrane) + stiffness.** Tile can't go directly over a flexing wood subfloor ⚠️ — it needs cement backer board or an uncoupling membrane (crack isolation) AND a stiff-enough floor structure (deflection cracks tile/grout ⚠️ — cross-ref Framing; the joists/subfloor must meet a stiffness standard). Over a bouncy floor, tile fails. A structural/substrate prerequisite, not just an underlayment choice.
3. **⚠️ Wet areas need waterproofing — the critical, failure-prone scope.** Bathroom floors and especially **showers need a waterproofing membrane + proper slope to drain** ⚠️ — NOT tile over backer (water passes through tile/grout) ⚠️. **Failed shower/wet-area waterproofing is a notorious, expensive hidden-leak/rot problem.** Cross-ref Plumbing — **assign ownership clearly** (tile setter vs. plumber vs. Caza). This is the highest-stakes part of bathroom tile.
4. **Pattern + tile size drive waste + labor.** Diagonal, herringbone, brick, and large-format/stone increase waste (~15%+) and labor (cutting, layout, handling). Straight-set standard tile is the baseline. Confirm the pattern/size — a big driver.
5. **⚠️ Stone needs sealing + special care.** Natural stone is porous (seal before grout + after ⚠️), variable (sort/dry-lay first), and some types etch/scratch (acidic cleaners, soft stone ⚠️). More material waste + skilled time. A premium scope.
6. **Thin-set + grout choice matters.** Modified vs. unmodified thin-set (per tile + membrane manufacturer ⚠️), and epoxy grout (stain/water-resistant, durable, harder to install) vs. cement grout (needs sealing). The right products for the application affect performance + labor.
7. **Layout/origin matters.** Tile is laid out from a planned origin (centered, balanced, avoiding thin slivers at walls) — a skilled planning step that affects the look + the cuts/waste. Poor layout = ugly slivers + more waste.
8. **Demo + disposal (replace).** Removing old tile (especially over a mortar bed) is heavy, dusty labor + disposal; old flooring removal varies by type. Real added scope on a replacement (and may reveal substrate issues ⚠️).
9. **⚠️ Heated floors (radiant) coordinate with tile.** Under-tile electric mats or hydronic radiant (cross-ref HVAC/Electrical) are installed in the setting bed ⚠️ — coordinate the system + the tile install (tile is the *best* flooring over radiant — good thermal mass). A real coordination + scope add if heated.
10. **⚠️ Silica dust (wet-cutting).** Cutting tile (especially dry) creates silica dust ⚠️ — wet-cut + dust control + PPE. A safety reality.
11. **Cure-gated schedule.** Thin-set cures before grout (often next day), grout before sealing/use — tile is a multi-day, sequenced job, not a one-day floor. Build the cure time into the schedule.
12. **Confirm substrate, waterproofing, pattern, stiffness, transitions** ⚠️ — the right tile job specifies the substrate prep + flatness ⚠️, the underlayment/system ⚠️, wet-area waterproofing ⚠️ (+ ownership), the pattern/size, floor stiffness ⚠️, and transitions — together. A tile quote without the substrate + waterproofing context is incomplete (and failure-prone).

## 1.8 Quick worked example

Bathroom floor tile, ~60 SF, porcelain, over wood subfloor, with a tile shower (illustrative):
- **Floor:** 60 SF + ~12% ≈ ~68 SF porcelain; **uncoupling/backer + thin-set**; **waterproofing** at the bathroom floor + **the shower (pan + walls + curb, sloped to drain)** ⚠️ — critical (cross-ref Plumbing — who owns it? ⚠️); **stiffness check** ⚠️.
- **Shower** ⚠️ is the big labor (waterproofing + slope + small tile/cuts) — separate from the simple floor.
- **Grout + seal** (cement grout → seal); **threshold/transition** at the door.
- **Labor:** prep/flatten + backer/membrane + **waterproofing** ⚠️ + set floor (60 × ~0.1 ≈ 6) + set shower (much more — cuts/slope/waterproofing) + grout + seal ≈ a **skilled multi-day, cure-gated** job; the shower + waterproofing dominate.
- **Equipment:** wet saw, trowels/floats, mixing drill, laser/level, knee pads + silica control ⚠️.
- *Key:* the **waterproofing + substrate flatness/stiffness** ⚠️ make or break it — not the tile. Assign shower waterproofing ownership clearly ⚠️.

# 2. HARDWOOD & ENGINEERED WOOD

## 2.1 System overview & types

Wood flooring — solid hardwood or engineered (a wood veneer over a stable plywood/composite core). Premium, refinishable (solid; some engineered), sensitive to moisture + movement ⚠️. The estimate is driven by **area (SF), solid vs. engineered (and the installation method — nail/staple, glue, or float), the substrate + moisture conditions ⚠️ (wood + moisture = trouble), acclimation ⚠️, the species/grade/width (wide planks move more ⚠️), the finish (prefinished vs. site-finished — a big difference), and expansion gaps** ⚠️.

**Types:**
- **Solid hardwood** ⚠️ — solid wood (oak, maple, hickory, etc.), nailed/stapled to a wood subfloor; refinishable many times; **moves with humidity** ⚠️ (not for below-grade/over-concrete-without-care ⚠️). Site-finished or prefinished.
- **Engineered wood** — real wood veneer over a plywood/composite core; **more dimensionally stable** ⚠️ (better for moisture/concrete/radiant ⚠️), can be nailed/glued/floated; refinishable a limited number of times (thin veneer = fewer). Versatile.
- **Prefinished** — finished at the factory (durable finish, no site finishing/dust/fumes, faster, ready to walk) — common.
- **Site-finished** — sanded + finished on site (seamless, custom stain/sheen, but dust/fumes/cure time + more labor).
- **Width/format** — strip / plank / wide-plank ⚠️ (wider = more movement/gaps ⚠️) / herringbone (premium labor).
- **Installation methods:** nail/staple-down (over wood subfloor), glue-down (over concrete/engineered), floating (engineered click — over underlayment, not fastened).

## 2.2 Required dimensions for an accurate quote

- **★ Area (SF)** — + waste (~10%, more for wide/pattern).
- **★ Solid vs. engineered** ⚠️ — drives method + moisture suitability.
- **★ Substrate + moisture conditions** ⚠️ — wood subfloor (nail) vs. concrete slab (glue/float + **moisture test** ⚠️) vs. existing; below/on/above grade ⚠️.
- **★ Installation method** — nail/staple, glue, or float (per type + substrate).
- **★ Finish** — prefinished vs. site-finished (big labor/scope difference).
- **★ Species / grade / width** — width (movement ⚠️), species (hardness/cost), grade (appearance).
- **★ Acclimation** ⚠️ — wood must acclimate to the space before install ⚠️ (time + conditions).
- **★ Subfloor flatness + condition** ⚠️ — flat, dry, sound ⚠️.
- **★ Radiant heat?** ⚠️ — engineered (and care) for radiant; solid generally not ⚠️ (cross-ref HVAC).
- **Transitions / thresholds / trim / shoe** — to other floors, walls (expansion gap ⚠️).
- **Stairs?** — wood stair treads (skilled, separate).

## 2.7 Best-practice notes that change material or labor

1. **⚠️ Moisture is wood's enemy — test + control it (the #1 best-practice).** Wood expands/contracts and cups/buckles/gaps with moisture ⚠️. **Test subfloor + slab moisture before install** ⚠️ (a concrete slab must be dry + have a vapor retarder; a damp crawlspace below threatens the floor). Solid wood is generally **not for below-grade/over-damp-concrete** ⚠️ — engineered is more tolerant. Skipping moisture testing/control is the leading cause of wood-floor failure. Non-negotiable.
2. **⚠️ Acclimate the wood before installing.** Wood must acclimate to the room's temperature/humidity for days before install ⚠️ — installing un-acclimated wood leads to gaps (it shrinks) or buckling (it expands). A real schedule item (material on site, conditioned, before the install day). Cheap to do, expensive to skip.
3. **⚠️ Expansion gaps at the perimeter — wood moves.** Leave an expansion gap around the perimeter (covered by shoe/trim) ⚠️ — wood needs room to expand seasonally, especially **wide planks (which move more) ⚠️** and in this climate (big seasonal humidity swings). No gap = buckling. A fundamental detail.
4. **Prefinished vs. site-finished is a big scope difference.** Prefinished = install-only, durable factory finish, no dust/fumes, ready to walk — faster, less disruptive. Site-finished = a seamless custom look + custom stain/sheen, but a **major added multi-day sand/finish/cure phase** (dust containment, fumes, no traffic during cure). A big cost + schedule + disruption difference — confirm which.
5. **⚠️ Solid vs. engineered by substrate + moisture + radiant.** Solid (nail to wood subfloor, refinishable many times, moves more, not for concrete/below-grade/most-radiant ⚠️) vs. engineered (more stable, glue/float/nail, good for concrete/below-grade/radiant ⚠️, limited refinishing). **Match to the substrate, moisture, and whether there's radiant heat** ⚠️ (cross-ref HVAC — engineered for radiant; solid generally not). A key material decision.
6. **⚠️ Subfloor flatness + soundness.** Wood flooring needs a flat, sound, clean subfloor ⚠️ — out-of-flat causes gaps, squeaks, and an uneven floor (self-leveler/sanding to correct). Loose/squeaky subfloor should be fixed first (cross-ref Framing). Prep is real labor.
7. **Width/species/grade affect cost + behavior.** Wide planks (more movement/gaps ⚠️, premium look), harder species (durability/cost), and grade (appearance/knots) drive material cost + install care. Patterns (herringbone) are premium labor.
8. **Demo + disposal (replace).** Removing old flooring (carpet, tile, old wood) varies; may reveal subfloor issues ⚠️ (and old adhesive/asbestos concerns in very old floors ⚠️ — test pre-1980s). Real added scope.
9. **⚠️ Radiant heat compatibility.** Over radiant (cross-ref HVAC), use engineered (stable) + follow the temperature limits ⚠️; solid wood generally isn't recommended (movement/gapping over heat). A material + coordination consideration if heated.
10. **Site-finish dust + fumes + cure.** Sanding creates fine dust (containment) and finishes have fumes + cure time (ventilation, no traffic) — disruptive in an occupied home. A real factor for site-finished.
11. **Transitions + stairs are skilled extras.** Transitions to other floors (reducers/thresholds) and wood stairs (treads/risers — skilled, per-step) are separate, real scopes. Stairs especially are slow, precise work.
12. **Confirm type, substrate, moisture, finish, acclimation, gaps** ⚠️ — the right wood job specifies solid vs. engineered, the substrate + moisture control ⚠️, the install method, prefinished vs. site-finished, acclimation ⚠️, expansion gaps ⚠️, and radiant compatibility ⚠️ — together. A wood-floor quote without the moisture + acclimation + substrate context is incomplete (and failure-prone).

## 2.8 Quick worked example

Engineered hardwood, ~400 SF living area, glue-down over a slab (illustrative, Zone 6):
- **Material:** 400 SF + ~10% ≈ ~440 SF engineered (stable — right choice over concrete ⚠️); **acclimate** on site ⚠️.
- **Substrate:** **moisture-test the slab** ⚠️ (critical), flatten if needed ⚠️, vapor retarder/moisture-control adhesive ⚠️; below-grade/on-grade favors engineered ⚠️.
- **Install:** glue-down; **expansion gap** at perimeter ⚠️ (covered by shoe/trim); transitions to adjacent floors.
- **Prefinished** (no site finishing) — install-only.
- **Labor:** moisture test + slab prep ⚠️ + glue-down (400 × ~0.07 ≈ 28 MH) + cuts/fit + trim/transitions/shoe ≈ a **skilled multi-day** job; prep + moisture control ⚠️ a real part.
- **Equipment:** trowel + adhesive tools, saws, moisture meter ⚠️, spacers/pull bar, laser/level.
- *Key:* **slab moisture testing/control + acclimation + the expansion gap** ⚠️ — engineered over concrete works *if* moisture is handled. Solid wood would be the wrong call here ⚠️.

# 3. RESILIENT FLOORING (LVP / LVT / SHEET VINYL / LAMINATE)

## 3.1 System overview & types

Resilient flooring — luxury vinyl plank/tile (LVP/LVT), sheet vinyl, and laminate. Popular, water-resistant (vinyl), affordable, DIY-friendly, fast-installing (much of it floating click-lock). The estimate is driven by **area (SF), the type (LVP/LVT vs. sheet vinyl vs. laminate — different install + water behavior ⚠️), the substrate + flatness ⚠️ (telegraphs through thin floating floors), the installation method (floating click vs. glue-down vs. loose-lay), underlayment, and expansion gaps (floating)** ⚠️. The fastest-growing, most popular flooring — especially water-resistant LVP.

**Types:**
- **Luxury vinyl plank/tile (LVP/LVT)** — rigid-core (SPC/WPC) or flexible; **water-resistant/waterproof** ⚠️ (great for baths/kitchens/basements ⚠️), durable, realistic wood/stone look, mostly **floating click-lock** (fast) or glue-down. The dominant modern choice. Rigid-core (SPC) is popular (stable, hides minor subfloor flaws better than thin laminate).
- **Sheet vinyl** — rolled vinyl, seamless (water-resistant ⚠️), economical, common in baths/laundry/utility; glued or loose-lay; fewer seams = good for wet areas.
- **Laminate** — printed photo layer over a fiberboard core; wood look, affordable, durable surface, but **NOT very water-resistant** ⚠️ (fiberboard core swells if wet ⚠️ — not for wet areas/basements unless water-resistant grade). Floating click-lock.
- **Vinyl composite tile (VCT)** — commercial; glued, waxed.

## 3.2 Required dimensions for an accurate quote

- **★ Type** — LVP/LVT (waterproof ⚠️) vs. sheet vinyl vs. laminate (water-sensitive ⚠️).
- **★ Substrate + flatness** ⚠️ — flatness telegraphs through thin floating floors ⚠️; over wood/concrete/existing.
- **★ Installation method** — floating click (most LVP/laminate), glue-down, loose-lay, or sheet.
- **★ Wet area / basement?** ⚠️ — favor waterproof vinyl ⚠️ (not laminate ⚠️).
- **★ Underlayment** — pad (floating), or attached-pad products; moisture barrier over concrete ⚠️.
- **★ Subfloor condition** ⚠️ — flat, clean, dry ⚠️.
- **★ Expansion gap (floating)** ⚠️ — perimeter gap for floating floors ⚠️.
- **Transitions / thresholds / trim** — to other floors.
- **Radiant heat?** ⚠️ — vinyl over radiant (temperature limits ⚠️).

## 3.7 Best-practice notes that change material or labor

1. **⚠️ Substrate flatness telegraphs through — prep still matters.** Thin floating floors (especially laminate + flexible LVP) **telegraph subfloor imperfections** ⚠️ — bumps/dips/debris show and cause clicking-joint stress or an uneven feel. **Flatten + clean the substrate** ⚠️ (rigid-core SPC hides minor flaws better, but flatness still matters). The prep is the main labor variable — don't skip it. (Resilient is forgiving to *install* but not immune to a bad substrate.)
2. **⚠️ Match water-resistance to the location — vinyl yes, laminate no.** LVP/LVT and sheet vinyl are water-resistant/waterproof ⚠️ (great for baths, kitchens, basements, mudrooms — water-prone here). **Laminate is NOT water-resistant** ⚠️ (its fiberboard core swells/ruins if wet) — keep it out of wet areas/basements unless it's a water-resistant grade. A critical material-selection rule; the wrong choice in a wet/below-grade spot fails. Basements especially favor waterproof vinyl here ⚠️.
3. **⚠️ Expansion gap for floating floors.** Floating LVP/laminate needs a perimeter expansion gap (covered by trim/shoe) ⚠️ — floating floors expand/contract with temperature and need room (and shouldn't be pinned by heavy cabinets ⚠️ — install flooring appropriately relative to cabinets). No gap = buckling/peaking. A fundamental floating-floor detail.
4. **Floating vs. glue-down vs. sheet.** Floating click (fast, DIY-friendly, replaceable, the LVP/laminate default) vs. glue-down (more stable underfoot, good for heavy traffic/rolling loads) vs. sheet (seamless, economical, good wet areas). Method affects labor + performance — confirm.
5. **Rigid-core (SPC) vs. flexible LVP.** Rigid-core (SPC/WPC) is dimensionally stable, hides minor subfloor flaws better, and feels solid; flexible/glue-down LVT conforms more (and shows substrate more). Rigid-core is popular for its forgiveness + stability. A product nuance affecting prep tolerance.
6. **Sheet vinyl needs careful templating.** Sheet vinyl is fit as one (or few) large piece(s) — careful templating/cutting around the room + sealed seams ⚠️ (in wet areas). Skilled cutting; few seams = good water performance. A different skill than plank.
7. **⚠️ Moisture barrier over concrete.** Over a slab, a moisture/vapor barrier ⚠️ (or a product with one built in) protects against slab moisture ⚠️ — even water-resistant vinyl benefits (moisture under it can cause issues/mold). Standard over concrete here.
8. **Demo + disposal (replace).** Removing old flooring varies; LVP often goes over some existing hard floors (if flat/sound) — or demo. Real (variable) scope; check for old-adhesive/asbestos concerns in very old resilient floors ⚠️ (pre-1980s — test).
9. **⚠️ Radiant compatibility (temperature limits).** Vinyl over radiant (cross-ref HVAC) must be a radiant-rated product within temperature limits ⚠️ — too hot damages/discolors vinyl. Confirm compatibility if heated.
10. **Transitions + heights.** Transitions to other floors (and matching heights — a thick tile vs. thin vinyl needs a transition) — plan thresholds/reducers. A real detail at doorways.
11. **Fast install = labor-light, prep-driven.** The big value: LVP/laminate installs fast (a room in hours) — so the labor estimate hinges on **substrate prep + cuts + room complexity**, not the laying itself. Estimate the prep honestly.
12. **Confirm type, water needs, substrate, method, gaps** ⚠️ — the right resilient job specifies the type (water needs ⚠️ — vinyl vs. laminate), substrate prep/flatness ⚠️, install method, moisture barrier (concrete) ⚠️, and expansion gaps (floating) ⚠️ — together. The most common mistake is laminate in a wet/below-grade spot ⚠️.

## 3.8 Quick worked example

Waterproof LVP, ~500 SF (kitchen + mudroom + half-bath), floating, over existing subfloor (illustrative, Zone 6):
- **Material:** 500 SF + ~10% ≈ ~550 SF **waterproof rigid-core LVP** ⚠️ (right for kitchen/mudroom/bath — water + snow/salt ⚠️); attached or separate pad.
- **Substrate:** flatten/clean ⚠️ (telegraphs ⚠️), patch as needed; moisture barrier if over concrete ⚠️.
- **Install:** floating click; **expansion gap** at perimeter ⚠️ (covered by shoe/trim); **transitions** to adjacent floors + thresholds; fit around cabinets/penetrations.
- **Labor:** substrate prep ⚠️ (the main variable) + floating install (500 × ~0.03 ≈ 15 MH) + cuts/fit + transitions/trim ≈ a **fast 1–2 person** job; prep + cuts the real variables.
- **Equipment:** utility knife/cutter, tapping block/pull bar/spacers, straightedge/square, knee pads.
- *Key:* **waterproof LVP is the right call** for these wet/snowy spaces ⚠️ (laminate would fail here ⚠️) — and the substrate prep, not the laying, drives the labor.

# 4. CARPET

## 4.1 System overview & types

Carpet — soft flooring installed over padding, stretched + tacked (stretch-in) or glued. Comfortable, warm, sound-absorbing, economical, common in bedrooms/basements/stairs. The estimate is driven by **area (SF or square yards), the carpet type/grade, the padding, the installation method (stretch-in over pad vs. glue-down), the substrate, seams (seam placement/quantity), and stairs (labor-intensive)** — plus a relatively quick, specialized install.

**Types:**
- **Cut pile (plush/Saxony/frieze/textured)** — cut yarn ends; soft, common residential; frieze (twisted) hides traffic/footprints.
- **Loop pile (Berber/level-loop)** — uncut loops; durable, hides traffic, good for high-traffic; Berber (chunky loops).
- **Cut-and-loop** — pattern/texture combos.
- **Fiber:** nylon (durable, resilient, common), polyester (soft, stain-resistant, less resilient), olefin/polypropylene (Berber/outdoor, moisture-resistant), wool (premium natural). Fiber affects durability/stain/cost.
- **Carpet tile** — modular squares (commercial/basements; glue/peel-stick; replaceable).
- **Padding (cushion)** — separate layer (density/thickness matters ⚠️ — too soft wears carpet out); the unsung performance + comfort component.

## 4.2 Required dimensions for an accurate quote

- **★ Area (SF or sq yd)** — + waste/seam planning (~10%, more for complex rooms/pattern-match).
- **★ Carpet type/grade/fiber** — durability + cost.
- **★ Padding type/thickness/density** ⚠️ — matched to carpet + traffic ⚠️.
- **★ Installation method** — stretch-in (over pad, tack strips) vs. glue-down (commercial/basement).
- **★ Substrate** — wood subfloor vs. concrete (glue or pad + tack); flat/clean ⚠️.
- **★ Seams** — room size vs. carpet roll width (12'/15') → seam placement + quantity (seams in traffic/sightlines = poor ⚠️).
- **★ Stairs?** — stair carpet (very labor-intensive, per-step) — waterfall vs. cap-and-band.
- **★ Transitions** — to hard floors (thresholds).
- **Room shapes / closets / complexity** — cuts + fitting.

## 4.7 Best-practice notes that change material or labor

1. **Padding is the unsung performance + comfort component — match it.** The pad (cushion) under carpet drives comfort, sound, AND carpet life ⚠️ — **too soft/thick a pad lets the carpet flex and wear out faster** (and voids some warranties); too thin feels hard. Match pad density/thickness to the carpet + traffic ⚠️. Cheap thin pad on good carpet is a false economy; the right pad makes carpet last. A real spec decision, not an afterthought.
2. **⚠️ Proper stretching prevents wrinkles/bubbles.** Stretch-in carpet must be properly power-stretched (not just knee-kicked) ⚠️ — under-stretched carpet wrinkles/buckles/bubbles over time (looks bad, trips). A power stretcher + skill is the difference between a lasting install and a wrinkled one. A workmanship best-practice; a cheap install often skimps on stretching.
3. **Seam placement + quantity matter.** Carpet comes in rolls (12'/15' wide) — rooms wider than the roll need seams ⚠️. **Place seams away from high-traffic + main sightlines, and minimize them** (good planning); poorly placed/many seams show + wear. Seam planning + heat-seaming is skilled labor. Roll width vs. room size drives the seam count.
4. **Stairs are very labor-intensive (per step).** Carpeting stairs (waterfall or the cleaner cap-and-band/upholstered) is slow, skilled, per-step work ⚠️ — far more labor per area than open floor. A staircase can rival a room in labor. Count + price stairs separately.
5. **Fiber + type to traffic/use.** Nylon (durable, resilient — high-traffic), polyester (soft, stain-resistant, less resilient — bedrooms), olefin (Berber/moisture — basements/outdoor), wool (premium). Loop/Berber + frieze hide traffic; plush shows footprints. Match fiber + style to the room's traffic + use. Affects cost + longevity.
6. **Substrate clean + sound.** Carpet needs a clean, sound, reasonably flat subfloor ⚠️ (it hides minor flatness better than hard floors, but debris/bumps show/wear; squeaky subfloor should be fixed first — cross-ref Framing). Prep is light but real.
7. **Demo + disposal (replace).** Pulling old carpet + pad + staples/tack strip (+ haul) is real (if light) labor + disposal — and may reveal subfloor issues ⚠️. Standard on a replacement.
8. **Carpet tile for basements/commercial.** Modular carpet tiles (glue/peel-stick) are replaceable (swap a stained one), good for basements (some moisture tolerance) + commercial — a different product/method worth offering for those uses.
9. **Transitions to hard floors.** Carpet-to-hard-floor transitions (thresholds, tack strip + edge) at doorways — plan them. A real detail.
10. **⚠️ Moisture in basements.** Carpet in below-grade/damp basements risks mold ⚠️ — favor moisture-tolerant pad/carpet tile, ensure the slab is dry (cross-ref Concrete), or steer to waterproof LVP (§3) instead. A cold-climate-basement caution.
11. **Relatively fast for simple rooms.** Open rectangular rooms go quickly (stretch-in) — the labor concentrates in stairs, seams, cuts, and complex shapes. Estimate those, not the open field.
12. **Confirm type, pad, seams, stairs, substrate** ⚠️ — the right carpet job specifies the carpet type/fiber, the matched padding ⚠️, seam planning, stairs (per step), and substrate condition ⚠️ — together. The most-overlooked items are the pad match ⚠️ and the stair labor.

## 4.8 Quick worked example

Bedroom carpet, ~3 bedrooms (~600 SF) + a staircase (13 steps), stretch-in (illustrative):
- **Material:** 600 SF + ~10% (seam/cut) ≈ ~660 SF carpet (nylon, residential) + **matched padding** ⚠️; **stairs** use extra carpet/pad.
- **Seams:** plan placement (away from traffic/sightlines ⚠️) per roll width; **tack strip** at perimeters.
- **Install:** **power-stretch** ⚠️ each room (prevent wrinkles), seam, trim; **stairs** (13 × per-step — the labor-intensive part); transitions to hard floors at doorways.
- **Labor:** prep + tack strip + pad + stretch/install rooms (600 × ~0.02 ≈ 12 MH) + seams + **stairs (13 × ~0.5 ≈ ~7 MH — significant)** + transitions ≈ a **specialized 1–2 person** job; the stairs + seams are much of it.
- **Equipment:** power stretcher + knee kicker, carpet knife, seaming iron, tack-strip/staple tools, stair tools, knee pads.
- *Key:* **match the pad ⚠️ + power-stretch properly ⚠️ + price the stairs** — the open bedrooms are fast; the stairs + seams + pad spec are where the job is.

# APPENDIX — Cross-system flooring principles

1. **Flooring estimates on area (SF) — but the substrate + prep decide success.** The finish is the visible part; **subfloor flatness, soundness, moisture control, and the right underlayment** ⚠️ determine whether the floor lasts. **Always assess the substrate + its condition** ⚠️ — most flooring failures are substrate/moisture failures, not the finish material. Prep is real, primary labor; a bid that ignores it is a red flag.
2. **⚠️ Moisture + flatness are the two universal enemies.** Moisture (slab moisture, below-grade damp, wood movement) ⚠️ ruins wood + laminate + adhesives + invites mold; out-of-flat substrate ⚠️ cracks tile, telegraphs through thin floors, and gaps wood. **Test moisture (concrete/wood) ⚠️ and correct flatness ⚠️** before installing. These two checks prevent most callbacks.
3. **⚠️ Match the material to the location's water exposure.** Tile + LVP/sheet vinyl = water-resistant (baths, kitchens, basements, mudrooms — water/snow/salt-prone here ⚠️); **laminate + solid wood are water-sensitive** ⚠️ (keep out of wet/below-grade areas). Wet-area tile + showers need **waterproofing** ⚠️ (cross-ref Plumbing — assign ownership). The most common selection mistake is a water-sensitive floor in a wet/below-grade spot.
4. **One combined difficulty factor, capped (~1.4×), on install labor.** Pattern, large-format/stone, site-finishing, stairs, complex rooms — pick the governing one. Substrate leveling ⚠️, moisture control ⚠️, waterproofing ⚠️, demo/disposal, stairs, site-finishing, and transitions are **separate line items**, not multipliers.
5. **Demo + disposal is real on replacements** — removing old flooring (heavy for tile/mortar-bed; light for carpet) + disposal, and it may reveal substrate problems ⚠️ (and old-adhesive/asbestos concerns pre-1980s — test ⚠️). Budget it.
6. **⚠️ Transitions + heights between materials.** Different floors have different thicknesses — plan transitions/thresholds at doorways (a thick tile to thin vinyl needs a transition) ⚠️. A real, easy-to-miss detail at every material change.
7. **⚠️ Radiant-floor compatibility** (cross-ref HVAC) — tile is ideal over radiant; engineered wood + radiant-rated vinyl work within temperature limits ⚠️; solid wood + laminate generally don't. Confirm compatibility if the floor is heated.
8. **Acclimation + cure + sequencing.** Wood acclimates (days) ⚠️; tile is cure-gated (thin-set → grout → seal, multi-day); site-finished wood has a finish-cure phase. **Flooring sequences late** (after drywall/paint, often after or coordinated with cabinetry/trim — though sometimes flooring-first under cabinets; confirm) and **before final plumbing/trim** (cross-ref Plumbing/Cabinetry). Build cure + acclimation + sequence into the schedule.
9. **Order attic stock + plan waste by pattern/material.** ~10% waste baseline; more for diagonal/herringbone/large-format/stone/pattern-match ⚠️. Always order extra for future repairs (dye-lot/discontinuation risk). A material-planning standard.
10. **Cross-references:** **subfloor stiffness/squeak + structure** ↔ Framing Manual ⚠️ (tile especially needs a stiff floor); **slab moisture/vapor barrier + frost/below-grade** ↔ Concrete Manual ⚠️; **wet-area/shower waterproofing** ↔ Plumbing Manual ⚠️ (assign ownership); **radiant-floor heat** ↔ HVAC Manual ⚠️; **flooring-vs-cabinet sequence + vanity/island** ↔ Cabinetry Manual; **transitions/trim/shoe + stairs** ↔ Carpentry; **dust/protection in occupied spaces + paint sequence** ↔ Interior Finish Manual. Confirm the *whole* assembly (substrate + moisture + finish + transitions + sequence), not just the surface.

*End of flooring manual. Built in the same format as the other build-trade manuals; the ⚠️ flags mark where the substrate, moisture, or waterproofing decision matters beyond laying the surface — assess what's under the floor and the moisture conditions, don't just price the finish.*
