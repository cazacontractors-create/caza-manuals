# Caza Contractors — Plumbing Estimating Manual (Scoping & Sub-Bid Reference)

**Purpose:** A trade-expert reference for the AI estimator to **scope plumbing work, understand what drives its cost, and evaluate a plumbing contractor's bid** — NOT an install how-to. For each area it defines what drives labor and material, the factors that move them, the material/fixture categories with rough units, the information needed for an accurate quote, and the code/permit realities — so Caza can scope plumbing on a job and judge a sub's number intelligently.

**⚠️ LICENSED TRADE — READ THIS FIRST.** Plumbing is a **licensed, permitted, inspected trade** — and for good reason: bad plumbing causes **water damage, contamination of drinking water (cross-connection/backflow ⚠️), sewage hazards, and (with gas water heaters/lines) explosion/CO risk ⚠️**. In most jurisdictions plumbing must be performed by a **licensed plumber**, under **permit**, and **inspected** ⚠️. Gas work (gas water heaters, lines) requires **gas licensing** ⚠️. **This manual does NOT provide installation instructions** — no pipe-joining/soldering procedures, no DWV design, no gas-connection how-to, no "rough it in yourself." It is an **estimating and sub-bid-checking reference.** Throughout, **⚠️ LICENSED/CODE** marks where a licensed plumber + permit + inspection + code compliance (and gas credentials where relevant) are required. **Caza subs plumbing to a licensed plumber** — this manual helps Caza scope the work and read the sub's bid, not do the install.

**Why this trade is framed like electrical/HVAC (sub-only for Caza):** As an exterior/structural contractor, Caza subs plumbing. The manual's value is **understanding the scope and cost drivers well enough to (1) coordinate the plumbing sub, (2) sanity-check the bid, (3) communicate scope to the customer, and (4) sequence the work** — not to perform it. The AI frames plumbing output as **"scope + estimated range + check the sub's bid,"** always noting it requires a licensed plumber + permit ⚠️.

**⚠️ WATER, GAS & HEALTH SAFETY:** Plumbing protects the potable water supply (backflow/cross-connection = contamination ⚠️), removes waste safely (DWV venting prevents sewer gas ⚠️), and (gas water heaters) involves fuel gas + combustion venting (explosion/CO ⚠️). These are health/life-safety systems — a core reason plumbing is licensed/inspected and why Caza subs it. The manual flags these realities; it does not instruct on them.

**Climate context (this market — cold, Climate Zone 6):** **Freeze protection dominates plumbing here.** Pipes that freeze burst and flood ⚠️ — so supply lines must be kept out of (or insulated/protected within) exterior walls and unconditioned spaces, hose bibs are frost-proof/freeze-resistant, well/service lines bury **below frost (~48"+ ⚠️)**, vacant/seasonal buildings get winterized, and crawlspace/unheated-area piping needs protection ⚠️. Rural Lewis County means **well + septic** are common (vs. municipal water/sewer) — a significant scope difference. Cross-ref Insulation (pipe protection) and Concrete (frost depth, septic/excavation).

**How to use this manual (for the AI):**
1. Identify the plumbing scope (supply/DWV rough-in, fixtures/trim, water heating, specialty/well-septic).
2. Use **Information Needed** to scope it; estimate conservatively + state assumptions + **always note it requires a licensed plumber + permit ⚠️**.
3. Estimate by **fixture count + rough-in complexity + new-vs-remodel + water heating + specialty (well/septic)**, giving a **range** (plumbing pricing varies widely by region/sub).
4. Frame output as **scope + estimated range + "verify with a licensed plumber's bid."**
5. Apply **Cost-Driver & Best-Practice Notes** to read a bid intelligently.

**Units key:** fixture = a plumbing fixture (sink, toilet, tub, etc.) · DWV = drain-waste-vent · rough-in = the in-wall/below-floor piping before fixtures · LF = linear foot (pipe runs) · GPM = gallons/min · gal = water-heater capacity · MH = man-hours · ⚠️ = licensed plumber + permit + inspection + code/gas — required, not DIY

**Estimating note:** Plumbing is priced largely by **fixture count** (each fixture = supply + drain + vent + trim) plus **rough-in complexity, new-vs-remodel** (the big swing), **water heating**, and **specialty (well/septic/gas)**. A common shorthand is **per "fixture" or per "rough-in"** plus the water heater and any specialty systems. Ranges below are rough scoping aids — **the licensed sub's bid is the real number**; this manual helps judge whether that number and scope are reasonable.

# 1. SUPPLY & DWV ROUGH-IN (WATER SUPPLY + DRAIN-WASTE-VENT)

## 1.1 Overview & what's involved

Rough-in is the in-wall/below-floor piping installed before walls close and before fixtures go in: the **water supply** (pressurized hot + cold to each fixture) and the **DWV (drain-waste-vent)** system (gravity drains + vents). **Licensed/permitted/inspected; the foundation of the plumbing job ⚠️.** Cost is driven by **the number of fixtures (each needs supply + drain + vent), the rough-in complexity + runs, new vs. remodel (the big swing — open framing vs. opening finished walls/floors), the pipe materials, and the DWV layout/venting** ⚠️.

**Scope elements (all ⚠️ licensed):**
- **Water supply (pressurized):**
  - **Service/main + distribution** — water enters (municipal main or well — §4) and distributes hot + cold to fixtures ⚠️.
  - **Supply piping** — PEX (flexible, common now, freeze-tolerant-ish ⚠️ but still protect), copper (durable, traditional), CPVC. Sized for flow ⚠️.
  - **Shutoffs / valves** — main, branch, fixture shutoffs.
  - **Hot water distribution** — from the water heater (§3); recirculation in larger homes.
- **DWV (drain-waste-vent) — gravity + venting:**
  - **Drains/waste** — carry waste to the sewer/septic by gravity ⚠️ (proper slope ⚠️). PVC/ABS common; cast iron (quiet/older).
  - **Vents** ⚠️ — vent piping (through the roof) lets drains flow and **keeps sewer gas out** ⚠️ (a drain without a vent siphons traps and lets sewer gas in — a health issue). Critical, code-governed ⚠️.
  - **Traps** — at each fixture (water seal blocks sewer gas).
  - **Main stack + building drain + sewer/septic connection** ⚠️.
- **Fixture rough-in** — stub-outs (supply + drain) located precisely for each fixture ⚠️.
- **Gas piping (if gas appliances)** ⚠️ — gas lines to water heater/range/etc. (licensed gas work ⚠️).

## 1.2 Information needed to scope / check a bid

- **★ Fixture count + types** — sinks, toilets, tubs/showers, dishwasher, washer, hose bibs, water heater, etc. (each = a rough-in). **The primary count.**
- **★ New construction vs. remodel/retrofit** ⚠️ — THE big labor driver (open framing fast; opening finished walls/floors slow).
- **★ Layout / fixture grouping** — fixtures stacked/grouped (efficient) vs. spread out (more piping); single vs. multi-story (stacks).
- **★ Pipe materials** — PEX vs. copper (supply); PVC/ABS vs. cast iron (DWV).
- **★ Water source + sewer/waste** — municipal vs. **well (§4)**; municipal sewer vs. **septic (§4)** (rural here).
- **★ DWV venting layout** ⚠️ — vents through roof (cross-ref Roofing — penetration/flashing); proper venting required ⚠️.
- **★ Gas piping** ⚠️ — gas appliances (water heater/range)? Licensed gas work ⚠️.
- **★ Building size / runs** — distances, stories, basement/slab (slab = harder).
- **★ Freeze protection** ⚠️ — keep supply out of exterior walls/unconditioned space; frost-proof hose bibs ⚠️ (cold-climate critical).
- **Wall/floor access (remodel)** — finished (open up) vs. accessible (basement/crawl).
- **Permit + inspection** ⚠️ — rough-in inspection before walls close ⚠️.

## 1.4 Labor / cost drivers (for scoping + judging the bid)

| Driver | Effect on cost |
|---|---|

| **Layout (grouped vs. spread; stories)** | Grouped/stacked fixtures = less pipe; spread/multi-story = more. |
| **Pipe material** | Copper > PEX in labor/material; cast iron > PVC. |

| **Gas piping** ⚠️ | Adds licensed gas scope. |

**Rough scoping (ranges — verify with the sub):** Plumbing rough-in is often scoped **per fixture** (a rough-in figure per sink/toilet/tub/etc.) plus the water heater and any specialty (well/septic/gas). **Remodel/retrofit costs materially more per fixture** (opening walls/floors). A whole-house rough-in is a multi-day licensed job. **The sub's bid is the number** — judge whether the per-fixture pricing and the new-vs-remodel difference look reasonable.

## 1.5 Cost-driver & best-practice notes (for reading the bid / coordinating)

1. **⚠️ Licensed, permitted, inspected — including a rough-in inspection before walls close.** Rough-in must be a licensed plumber's work under permit, with a **rough-in inspection before insulation/drywall** ⚠️. **Sequence: plumbing rough-in + inspection before insulation and drywall** (and plumbing/HVAC rough-ins usually go before electrical). Closing walls before the rough-in inspection means tearing them back open. A key scheduling/coordination point for Caza.
2. **New vs. remodel is the biggest cost driver.** Open-framing new work is fast; **opening finished walls/floors to add/move plumbing is slow + disruptive** (demo, fishing pipe, patching — cross-ref Drywall/Flooring) ⚠️. Moving plumbing (relocating a kitchen/bath) is especially costly. A remodel bid should reflect the disruption; if priced like new work, question it.
3. **⚠️ DWV venting is critical (sewer gas + drainage) and code-governed.** Every fixture's drain needs proper venting ⚠️ — vents (through the roof) let drains flow and **keep sewer gas out of the house** ⚠️. Improper/missing venting causes slow drains, gurgling, siphoned traps, and sewer gas (a health hazard). A code-inspected fundamental — a cheap bid that skimps on venting is a red flag (and fails inspection). Vents penetrate the roof (cross-ref Roofing — flashing/sealing).
4. **⚠️ Freeze protection — the cold-climate non-negotiable.** Supply pipes in exterior walls or unconditioned spaces **freeze and burst** here ⚠️ — flooding the house. Best practice: keep supply piping within the conditioned envelope (not in exterior walls), insulate/protect any in cold zones (cross-ref Insulation), use **frost-proof hose bibs** ⚠️, and bury service/well lines below frost (~48"+ ⚠️). A bid for this market should address freeze protection; ignoring it is a real risk. Seasonal/vacant buildings need winterizing.
5. **Fixture grouping/layout affects cost (a design point).** Stacking baths (over each other), grouping fixtures, and placing them near the water heater/stack minimizes piping + runs + cost. Spread-out fixtures and long runs cost more. Relevant in design/addition — a good plan saves plumbing money. (Caza can flag this at the design stage.)
6. **Pipe material tradeoffs.** PEX (flexible, fast, freeze-tolerant-ish ⚠️ — but still protect, fewer fittings, cheaper labor) vs. copper (durable, traditional, premium, more labor); PVC/ABS (common DWV) vs. cast iron (quieter, premium, older homes). Material choice affects cost + longevity; PEX is common modern residential.
7. **⚠️ Backflow / cross-connection protection (potable water safety).** The potable water supply must be protected from contamination (backflow preventers where required — hose bibs, irrigation, boilers, etc.) ⚠️ — a health-critical code item. A good plumber handles it; relevant with irrigation, boilers, or certain fixtures.
8. **Slab vs. accessible.** Plumbing in/under a slab (cross-ref Concrete — set before/in the pour) or in a finished space is harder than in an accessible basement/crawl. Slab rough-in must be coordinated with the concrete (sequencing — pipe before pour). Access drives cost.
9. **⚠️ Gas piping is licensed gas work.** Gas lines (to a gas water heater, range, etc.) require licensed gas work ⚠️ (leak/explosion risk) — confirm the plumber/contractor has gas credentials. Coordinate with HVAC (shared gas/fuel) and the appliance scope.
10. **Coordinate rough-in with framing/HVAC/electrical/finishes.** Plumbing rough-in happens after framing, often before/with HVAC and before electrical, and before insulation/drywall — Caza sequences the subs (and notes that big DWV/stacks need framing space — cross-ref Framing). A real multi-trade coordination point. Slab plumbing coordinates with concrete.
11. **Future-proofing during rough-in is cheap.** Adding a stub-out for a future fixture (a future bath, a utility sink, an outdoor kitchen) while walls are open is far cheaper than later — worth discussing if the customer has future plans.
12. **Permit + rough-in + final inspections** ⚠️ — confirm the sub pulls the permit and both inspections happen (rough-in before close-up, final at trim). **No permit/inspection = major red flag** (water damage, sewer gas, contamination, Caza/homeowner liability). Non-negotiable.

# 2. FIXTURES & TRIM-OUT

## 2.1 Overview & what's involved

Trim-out (or "finish") is setting and connecting the visible plumbing after walls/floors are finished: **toilets, sinks/faucets, tubs/showers, dishwashers, disposals, washer hookups, hose bibs, water heater connections, and final testing.** Counted per fixture. **Licensed/permitted, with a final inspection ⚠️.** Cost is driven by **the fixture count + types, fixture complexity/quality (a basic sink vs. a multi-valve shower system or a freestanding tub), customer- vs. sub-supplied fixtures, and connecting/testing.**

**Scope elements (all ⚠️ licensed):**
- **Toilets** — set + connect (wax/seal, supply, shutoff). Standard, comfort-height, one-piece, wall-hung (more complex), bidet.
- **Sinks + faucets** — kitchen, bath, vanity, utility — set + connect supply + drain/trap. Faucet quality/type varies.
- **Tubs / showers** ⚠️ — tub set, shower valve + trim, **shower pan/waterproofing** ⚠️ (tile showers — cross-ref Flooring/tile; a critical waterproofing scope), tub/shower surrounds, multi-valve/body-spray systems (complex), freestanding tubs (premium).
- **Dishwasher / disposal / icemaker** — appliance connections (supply, drain, sometimes electrical coordination).
- **Washer hookups** — supply box + drain.
- **Hose bibs / outdoor** ⚠️ — frost-proof (cold climate ⚠️).
- **Water heater connections (§3)** — connect the heater.
- **Shutoffs, supply lines, P-traps, escutcheons** — the finish connection parts.
- **Connect + test** ⚠️ — leak-test, function-test, set; final inspection ⚠️.

## 2.2 Information needed to scope / check a bid

- **★ Fixture count + types** — toilets, sinks, tubs/showers, dishwasher, disposal, washer, hose bibs, etc.
- **★ Fixture complexity/quality** — basic vs. high-end/complex (multi-valve showers, freestanding tubs, wall-hung toilets).
- **★ Fixtures supplied by whom** — customer-supplied vs. sub-supplied (affects material in the bid + coordination/defect/schedule risk).
- **★ Shower/tub type** ⚠️ — prefab unit vs. **tile shower (pan + waterproofing ⚠️ — cross-ref Flooring/tile)**; the waterproofing is critical.
- **★ Appliance connections** — dishwasher, disposal, icemaker, washer (+ any electrical coordination — cross-ref Electrical).
- **★ Special fixtures** — bidets, body sprays, pot fillers, instant-hot, water filtration (§4), etc.
- **Accessibility fixtures** — grab bars, ADA/comfort fixtures, walk-in tubs.
- **Final inspection** ⚠️ — trim triggers the final plumbing inspection ⚠️.

| Driver | Effect on cost |

**Rough scoping (ranges — verify with the sub):** Trim-out is often scoped **per fixture** (a set-and-connect figure per fixture, more for complex/high-end/tile-shower work). **The sub's bid is the number** — judge whether the per-fixture pricing + complexity adders look reasonable, and **clarify who supplies fixtures** (a major bid variable).

## 2.5 Cost-driver & best-practice notes (for reading the bid / coordinating)

1. **⚠️ Licensed work + final inspection at trim.** Fixtures must be set/connected by the licensed plumber, and trim triggers the **final plumbing inspection** ⚠️ (leak test, function, proper connections). Part of the permitted job. Confirm the final inspection happens.
2. **Fixture complexity drives trim labor far more than count alone.** A basic toilet/sink is quick; a multi-valve/body-spray shower system, freestanding tub, wall-hung toilet, or pot filler is much more labor. A bid should reflect the *mix*, not a flat per-fixture number for everything. High-end/complex fixtures legitimately cost more.
3. **⚠️ Tile showers need a waterproofed pan/system — a critical scope (and failure point).** A tile shower requires a properly built, **waterproofed shower pan + walls** ⚠️ (membrane/system) — NOT just tile over backer ⚠️. **Failed shower waterproofing is a notorious, expensive hidden-leak/rot problem** (cross-ref Flooring/tile). This is plumbing + tile coordination (the plumber sets the drain/valve; the waterproofing + tile may be plumber, tile setter, or Caza — clarify who owns it ⚠️). A prefab shower unit avoids it but looks different. **Confirm who is responsible for the shower waterproofing** — a common gap that causes leaks + finger-pointing.
4. **Clarify customer-supplied vs. sub-supplied fixtures — a major bid variable.** Customer-supplied = labor-only to install (cheaper bid material, but coordination + defect/wrong-item risk + schedule risk if late/damaged); sub-supplied = in the bid (markup, sub owns supply). **Pin this down** — fixtures are a big cost and a common source of bid confusion, delays, and finger-pointing. Customer-supplied fixtures arriving late/damaged/incomplete stall trim-out.
5. **⚠️ Frost-proof hose bibs (cold climate).** Outdoor hose bibs must be frost-proof/freeze-resistant here ⚠️ (and pitched to drain) — standard bibs freeze and burst. A small but real cold-climate item.
6. **Appliance connections + electrical coordination.** Dishwashers, disposals, icemakers, and washers need plumbing connections AND often electrical (cross-ref Electrical — dedicated circuits/outlets) — coordinate the two trades. A common gap (plumbing roughed for a dishwasher but no electrical, or vice versa).
7. **Trim-out is late — coordinate with finishes.** Trim happens after walls/floors/tile are finished (fixtures go on finished surfaces) — coordinate near the end, after tile/flooring/paint (cross-ref Flooring/Drywall). Sequencing point: the tile shower must be done before the shower trim; vanities/counters before the sink (cross-ref Cabinetry).
8. **Protect finishes during trim.** Setting fixtures in a finished bath/kitchen means protecting new floors/counters/walls — a real care factor (a dropped wrench on a new tub or a leak test gone wrong damages finishes).
9. **Special/accessibility fixtures.** Bidets, pot fillers, instant-hot, water filtration (§4), and accessibility fixtures (grab bars, walk-in tubs, ADA-height) are premium/specialty installs — real adders if in scope. Relevant for aging-in-place or high-end work.
10. **Permit + final inspection + leak testing** ⚠️ — confirm the permit, final inspection, and that the system is leak/function tested. **No permit/inspection = red flag.** A good plumber tests before declaring done (a hidden leak found later is a disaster in a finished space).

# 3. WATER HEATING

## 3.1 Overview & what's involved

Water heating supplies domestic hot water. **Licensed/permitted; gas units add gas + combustion venting (explosion/CO ⚠️).** Cost is driven by **the type (tank vs. tankless), the fuel/energy source (gas/propane/electric/heat-pump), the capacity/sizing, new vs. replacement, venting/fuel/electrical needs, and code-required safety (T&P relief, expansion, pan, seismic/strapping where applicable)** ⚠️.

**Types:**
- **Storage tank — gas/propane:** Most common; burns gas to heat a tank. Needs gas ⚠️ + combustion venting ⚠️. Atmospheric vs. power-vent vs. direct-vent (venting type matters ⚠️).
- **Storage tank — electric:** Electric elements heat a tank; simple, no venting, but higher operating cost; heavy electrical draw ⚠️ (cross-ref Electrical).
- **Tankless ("on-demand") — gas/propane:** Heats water on demand, no tank — endless hot water, space-saving, efficient, higher upfront; needs **substantial gas + venting** ⚠️ (and often a gas-line upgrade ⚠️) or, electric tankless = very heavy electrical ⚠️.
- **Heat-pump water heater (HPWH):** Electric heat pump (very efficient — pulls heat from air); higher upfront, big operating savings, needs space/air + a condensate drain ⚠️; cools/dehumidifies the space it's in (a basement consideration in a cold climate ⚠️). Electrical ⚠️.
- **Indirect (with a boiler):** A tank heated by the hydronic boiler (cross-ref HVAC) — efficient if there's a boiler.
- **Point-of-use / recirculation:** Small under-sink units; recirculation for instant hot at far fixtures.

## 3.2 Information needed to scope / check a bid

- **★ Type** — tank vs. tankless vs. heat-pump vs. indirect.
- **★ Fuel/energy** — gas/propane (venting + gas ⚠️) vs. electric (capacity ⚠️) vs. heat-pump (space/air + condensate ⚠️) vs. indirect (boiler).
- **★ Capacity / sizing** — household size + demand (tank gallons or tankless GPM); proper sizing ⚠️.
- **★ New vs. replacement** — replacing existing (reuse venting/gas/electrical?) vs. new (or switching type — e.g. tank→tankless = venting/gas changes ⚠️).
- **★ Venting + gas** ⚠️ — gas units: venting type + gas supply (tankless often needs a bigger gas line ⚠️).
- **★ Electrical** ⚠️ — electric/heat-pump units: capacity/circuit (cross-ref Electrical) ⚠️.
- **★ Location + space** — heat-pump needs air/space + condensate ⚠️; tankless wall-mount; pan/drain needs.
- **★ Code safety** ⚠️ — T&P relief valve + discharge, expansion tank (closed systems), drain pan, etc. ⚠️.
- **Recirculation** — instant hot at far fixtures (larger homes).
- **Permit + inspection** ⚠️.

## 3.3 Equipment / material categories (for understanding/checking the bid)

| Category | Unit | Notes (sub installs — licensed ⚠️) |
|---|---|---|
| **Water heater (tank/tankless/HPWH/indirect)** | EA | Sized ⚠️; type/fuel/efficiency + brand drive cost. |
| **Venting (gas: B-vent/power-vent/direct-vent/PVC)** ⚠️ | LF/EA | Type per unit ⚠️ (CO safety ⚠️). |
| **Gas supply + connection (+ possible line upgrade for tankless)** ⚠️ | LF/EA | Licensed gas ⚠️. |
| **Electrical connection / circuit (electric/HPWH)** ⚠️ | EA | Cross-ref Electrical ⚠️. |
| **T&P relief valve + discharge + expansion tank + pan/drain** ⚠️ | EA | Code safety ⚠️. |
| **Connections / shutoffs / supply lines** | EA | |
| **Condensate handling (HPWH/condensing)** ⚠️ | LF/EA | Drain/pump ⚠️. |
| **Recirculation pump + line (if used)** | EA/LF | Larger homes. |
| **Permit fee** ⚠️ | EA | Always. |

## 3.4 Labor / cost drivers (for scoping + judging the bid)

| Driver | Effect on cost |
|---|---|
| **Type** | Tank (simplest) → tankless/HPWH (more — venting/gas/electrical/space) → indirect. |
| **Fuel/energy** ⚠️ | Gas (venting + gas) vs. electric (capacity) vs. heat-pump (space/condensate). |
| **New vs. replacement** | Like-for-like swap (modest) vs. switching type (venting/gas/electrical changes — more). |
| **Venting + gas (gas units)** ⚠️ | New/changed venting, gas line (tankless often needs upgrade ⚠️). |
| **Electrical (electric/HPWH)** ⚠️ | Circuit/capacity (cross-ref Electrical) ⚠️. |
| **Capacity/sizing** ⚠️ | Bigger demand = bigger unit. |
| **Code safety (T&P/expansion/pan)** ⚠️ | Required items. |
| **Location/space (HPWH/tankless)** | Space + condensate + access. |
| **Permit + inspection** ⚠️ | Always. |

**Rough scoping (ranges — verify with the sub):** A **like-for-like tank replacement** is a defined modest job (unit + connections + code items + permit); **switching to tankless or heat-pump** is more (venting/gas/electrical changes ⚠️, possible gas-line or circuit upgrade). **The sub's bid is the number** — judge whether the scope (venting, gas/electrical, code safety) is complete.

## 3.5 Cost-driver & best-practice notes (for reading the bid / coordinating)

1. **⚠️ Licensed/permitted; gas units = gas + venting safety (explosion/CO).** Water heater work is licensed plumbing under permit + inspection ⚠️; gas units involve gas (licensed ⚠️) + combustion venting (CO ⚠️). Caza subs it. Confirm gas credentials + permit for gas units.
2. **⚠️ Switching type changes venting/gas/electrical — not a simple swap.** Tank→tankless (gas) often needs **new venting + a bigger gas line** ⚠️; going to electric/heat-pump needs **electrical capacity/a circuit** ⚠️ (cross-ref Electrical). A bid that prices a type-switch like a like-for-like swap is missing scope. Like-for-like replacement is the simple/cheap case.
3. **Type tradeoffs (upfront vs. operating vs. space).** Tank (cheap upfront, standby loss, takes space, finite hot water); tankless (endless hot water, efficient, space-saving, higher upfront + gas/venting ⚠️); heat-pump (very efficient, big operating savings, higher upfront, needs space/air + condensate, cools the space ⚠️); indirect (efficient with a boiler). A payback + space + fuel decision worth presenting to the customer.
4. **⚠️ Heat-pump water heater in a cold climate — the space-cooling catch.** HPWHs are very efficient but **pull heat from (and cool/dehumidify) the air around them** ⚠️ — in a cold-climate basement, that's "free" dehumidification in summer but **steals heat in winter** (the space's heating system makes up for it). A real consideration here — best in a space with excess heat/humidity, less ideal in a tightly-heated basement. Worth flagging, not a dealbreaker.
5. **⚠️ Code safety items are required (T&P, expansion, pan).** A T&P (temperature & pressure) relief valve + proper discharge, an expansion tank (on closed systems), and a drain pan (where leaks would damage) are code-required safety ⚠️ — a water heater can be dangerous (over-pressure) without them. A bid missing these is a red flag (and fails inspection).
6. **Sizing to demand.** Tank gallons / tankless GPM sized to household size + simultaneous demand ⚠️ — undersized = running out of hot water; oversized tank = wasted standby energy. Proper sizing matters (a quality signal).
7. **⚠️ Electrical capacity (electric/tankless-electric/HPWH).** Electric tank, electric tankless (very heavy draw ⚠️), and HPWH need adequate circuits/capacity (cross-ref Electrical §1 — possible service upgrade) ⚠️. Electric tankless especially is a big electrical load. Coordinate with the electrical sub.
8. **Venting type matters (gas).** Atmospheric (chimney), power-vent (fan, more flexible), direct-vent (sealed combustion — good for tight houses ⚠️ — cross-ref HVAC combustion-air), or condensing (PVC). The venting type affects cost + suitability (a tight house favors sealed/direct-vent ⚠️). 
9. **Recirculation for far fixtures.** In larger homes, a recirculation system delivers instant hot water at distant fixtures (comfort + water savings) — an add worth mentioning for big/spread layouts.
10. **Permit + inspection + safety items** ⚠️ — confirm the permit, inspection, and the code safety items (T&P/expansion/pan/venting). **A missing-permit, missing-safety-item water heater is a red flag** (over-pressure, CO, water damage).

# 4. SPECIALTY & SYSTEMS (WELL, SEPTIC, GAS, WATER TREATMENT, OUTDOOR)

## 4.1 Overview & what's involved

Beyond standard supply/DWV/fixtures — especially relevant **rurally (Lewis County)**: **wells (private water supply), septic systems (private waste), gas piping, water treatment/filtration, sump/drainage, and outdoor/irrigation.** Mostly **licensed/permitted/specialized** ⚠️ (wells + septic are often their own specialists + heavily regulated by health departments ⚠️). Cost is driven by **the specific system, its size/site conditions, and code/health-department requirements** ⚠️.

**Scope elements:**
- **Private well (water supply)** ⚠️: Drilled well + pump (submersible/jet) + pressure tank + controls + line to house (below frost ⚠️). Well drilling = a specialist (driller); the plumber connects + sets the pump/tank/controls. Water quality testing ⚠️ (health). A major rural scope. ⚠️
- **Septic system (private waste)** ⚠️: Septic tank + drain/leach field — **designed + permitted by the health department ⚠️** (soil/perc test, sizing, setbacks) — a heavily regulated, engineered, excavation-heavy scope ⚠️. Installer often a septic specialist + excavation (cross-ref Concrete/excavation). Replacing/repairing a failed system is common. A major rural scope. ⚠️
- **Gas piping** ⚠️: Distribution to gas appliances (water heater, range, furnace/boiler — cross-ref HVAC, fireplace, generator — cross-ref Electrical, grill) — licensed gas work ⚠️; propane (tank + supply, rural) or natural gas. Sizing for load ⚠️.
- **Water treatment / filtration** ⚠️: Softeners (hard well water — common here), filtration (sediment/iron/sulfur — common well issues ⚠️), UV (bacteria), RO (drinking) — sized to water quality (test first ⚠️). Common with wells.
- **Sump pump / drainage** ⚠️: Sump pit + pump (basement/foundation water — cross-ref Concrete foundation drainage), backup (battery/water-powered) for outages. Cold-climate discharge (freeze ⚠️).
- **Outdoor / irrigation** ⚠️: Hose bibs (frost-proof ⚠️), irrigation (backflow ⚠️ + winterization ⚠️), outdoor kitchens, pool/spa fill.
- **Backflow prevention** ⚠️: Where required (irrigation, boilers, commercial) — protects potable supply ⚠️.

## 4.2 Information needed to scope / check a bid

- **★ Which system(s)** — well, septic, gas, treatment, sump, irrigation.
- **★ Municipal vs. private (well/septic)** ⚠️ — rural = often private (major added scope).
- **★ Well:** new (drill — specialist) vs. existing (pump/tank/treatment); depth/yield; water quality test ⚠️.
- **★ Septic:** new (design + perc test + health-dept permit ⚠️ + excavation) vs. repair/replace; tank + field sizing ⚠️; site/soil.
- **★ Gas:** appliances served + load ⚠️; propane (tank) vs. natural gas; licensed gas ⚠️.
- **★ Water quality (treatment)** ⚠️ — test results (hardness/iron/sulfur/bacteria) drive treatment ⚠️.
- **★ Sump/drainage** — basement water issues (cross-ref Concrete); backup needs; freeze-safe discharge ⚠️.
- **★ Site conditions** — excavation, setbacks, frost depth (~48"+ ⚠️), access.
- **★ Health-department / regulatory** ⚠️ — well/septic permits + testing (heavily regulated) ⚠️.
- **Permit + inspection** ⚠️.

This manual helps Caza understand the scope + coordinate (and Caza's excavation/concrete capability may tie in).

## 4.5 Cost-driver & best-practice notes (for reading the bid / coordinating)

1. **⚠️ Well + septic are heavily regulated, specialized, major scopes (rural reality here).** Private wells (drilling specialist + water quality testing ⚠️) and septic systems (health-department-designed + permitted, soil/perc test, sizing, setbacks ⚠️ + excavation) are **major, regulated scopes** ⚠️ — often dedicated specialists, not a general plumber, with a **health-department process + timeline** ⚠️. Common in Lewis County (no municipal water/sewer in much of it). Caza coordinates (and the excavation may tie to Caza's capability — cross-ref Concrete). Identify these early; they drive cost + schedule.
2. **⚠️ Septic = soil/perc test + health-dept design first.** A septic system's size + type + feasibility depend on a **soil/percolation test + health-department-approved design** ⚠️ (sized to the home + soil + setbacks). You can't price a septic system without knowing the soil + design ⚠️. A failed/undersized field is a major, costly problem. A new build or a failed system needs this process up front — a real timeline + cost driver.
3. **⚠️ Well water quality testing drives treatment.** Well water commonly has hardness, iron, sulfur ("rotten egg"), sediment, or bacteria ⚠️ — **test the water** ⚠️ to size treatment (softener, filtration, UV, RO). Common here. A well scope should include testing + appropriate treatment; "just drill a well" without treatment may deliver bad water. Treatment is a real, often-needed add with wells.
4. **⚠️ Below-frost burial (well/service/septic lines).** Well lines, water service, and septic components must be below frost (~48"+ ⚠️) or they freeze/heave (cross-ref Concrete frost depth). A cold-climate excavation requirement — part of the scope here.
5. **⚠️ Gas piping is licensed + sized to load.** Gas distribution (to water heater, range, HVAC, fireplace, generator, grill) is licensed gas work ⚠️ sized to the total appliance load ⚠️ — propane (tank + supply, rural) or natural gas. Coordinate with HVAC (shared gas), the appliance scope, and the generator (cross-ref Electrical). Adding appliances may need a bigger line/tank ⚠️.
6. **Sump/drainage ties to foundation water (cross-ref Concrete).** A sump pit + pump handles basement/foundation water (cross-ref Concrete foundation drainage) — with a **battery/water-powered backup** for power outages (the pump's useless in an outage without it) and a **freeze-safe discharge** ⚠️ (cold-climate — a frozen discharge backs up). A real basement-water scope, often paired with foundation/drainage work Caza does.
7. **⚠️ Backflow / cross-connection protection.** Irrigation, boilers, outdoor connections, and commercial work need backflow prevention ⚠️ to protect the potable supply from contamination — a health-critical code item. Required where applicable; a good plumber handles it.
8. **⚠️ Irrigation winterization (cold climate).** Irrigation systems must be winterized (blown out) each fall here ⚠️ or the lines freeze/burst — a seasonal service + a design consideration. Outdoor plumbing generally needs freeze planning ⚠️.
9. **Water treatment sized to the water + use.** Softeners (hard water), sediment/iron/sulfur filtration, UV (bacteria), RO (drinking) — sized to the test results + household ⚠️. Common with wells here. A real scope; matching treatment to the actual water quality is the quality signal.
10. **Permit(s) + health-dept + qualified specialist** ⚠️ — confirm permits, health-department approval (well/septic), and the right specialist (well driller, septic installer, licensed gas). **No permit / health-dept approval / qualified installer = major red flag** (contamination, septic failure, gas safety, regulatory + resale problems — an unpermitted septic is a serious liability).

# APPENDIX — Cross-system plumbing principles (for scoping & checking bids)

1. **⚠️ Plumbing is a licensed, permitted, inspected trade — Caza subs it.** Supply/DWV, fixtures, water heating, and specialty systems require a **licensed plumber + permit + inspection + code compliance**, plus **gas licensing** (gas work ⚠️) and **health-department approval** (well/septic ⚠️). This manual is for **scoping, coordinating, and checking the sub's bid** — NOT for performing plumbing or as an install how-to. The estimate = the qualified sub's bid + Caza coordination/markup, always noting the licensed-plumber + permit requirement ⚠️. Frame output as **scope + estimated range + "verify with a licensed plumber's bid."**
2. **Estimate on fixture count + rough-in complexity + new-vs-remodel + water heating + specialty.** Plumbing cost is driven by **fixture count** (each = supply + drain + vent + trim), **rough-in complexity**, the **new-vs-remodel multiplier** (opening finished walls/floors is the big swing), **water heating**, and **specialty (well/septic/gas)**. Give **ranges**, not false precision — regional/sub pricing varies widely.
3. **⚠️ Freeze protection dominates in this cold climate.** Pipes freeze and burst here ⚠️ — keep supply out of exterior walls/unconditioned space (cross-ref Insulation), use frost-proof hose bibs ⚠️, bury well/service/septic lines below frost (~48"+ ⚠️ — cross-ref Concrete), winterize seasonal/vacant buildings + irrigation ⚠️. A bid for this market should address freeze protection; ignoring it is a real risk.
4. **⚠️ Rural = well + septic (major regulated scopes).** Much of Lewis County is on private well + septic (not municipal) ⚠️ — major, specialized, health-department-regulated scopes (drilling, excavation, soil/perc test, design, water testing) ⚠️ often handled by specialists. Identify early; they drive cost + schedule. Caza's excavation/concrete capability may tie in.
5. **⚠️ Health + safety systems: potable protection, DWV venting, gas.** Backflow/cross-connection (drinking water contamination ⚠️), DWV venting (sewer gas + drainage ⚠️), and gas (explosion/CO ⚠️) are health/life-safety — core reasons plumbing is licensed/inspected and Caza subs it. The manual flags these; it doesn't instruct on them.
6. **New vs. remodel is the biggest labor swing** — open framing (run it) vs. opening finished walls/floors (demo, fish, patch — cross-ref Drywall/Flooring) ⚠️. Moving plumbing (relocating a kitchen/bath) is especially costly. A remodel bid should reflect the disruption; if priced like new work, question it.
7. **⚠️ Sequencing + coordination is Caza's job.** Plumbing rough-in happens **after framing, often before/with HVAC and before electrical, and before insulation/drywall** (with a **rough-in inspection before close-up** ⚠️); slab plumbing coordinates with the concrete pour; trim-out happens **after tile/flooring/cabinetry/paint** (near the end). Appliance connections coordinate with electrical. **Caza coordinates the plumber into the project sequence** — closing walls before inspection = tear-out.
8. **Clarify fixture/material supply (customer vs. sub).** Customer-supplied fixtures = labor-only install (+ coordination/defect/schedule risk); sub-supplied = in the bid (+ markup). Pin it down — fixtures are a big cost and a common bid-confusion + finger-pointing source.
9. **⚠️ The shower-waterproofing gap — assign it clearly.** Tile showers need a waterproofed pan/system ⚠️ (cross-ref Flooring/tile) — a notorious hidden-leak failure point. **Clarify who owns the shower waterproofing** (plumber, tile setter, or Caza) — a common gap that causes leaks + finger-pointing. Prefab units avoid it.
10. **Permit + inspection are non-negotiable red-flag checks** ⚠️. Confirm the sub pulls the permit and the work is inspected (rough-in + final), with health-department approval for well/septic. **No permit/inspection = major red flag** — water damage, sewer gas, contamination, septic failure, gas safety, and Caza/homeowner liability (an unpermitted septic or water heater is a serious resale + safety problem). The single most important bid-quality check.

Coordinate the plumbing sub with the trades it touches; confirm the *whole* job (rough-in + fixtures + water heating + specialty + safety + sequence), not just the pipe.
