# Caza Contractors — Cabinetry & Countertops Estimating Manual

**Purpose:** A trade-expert reference for the AI estimator covering cabinetry and countertops — kitchen cabinets, countertops (the fabricated work surface), bath vanities, and built-ins/specialty millwork. Same structure as the build-trade manuals: for each system it defines the labor tasks that drive time, the factors that move them, the material/component list with units, special equipment, the dimensions required for a quote, and the best-practices that change material or labor.

**Cabinetry estimates differently from area-based trades — it's counted by the box (cabinet) + the countertop (SF) + the install precision.** The cabinets themselves are usually *bought* (stock/semi-custom/custom — a major material cost the supplier/manufacturer drives), and the contractor's scope is often **layout, install, and coordination** — setting boxes level/plumb/square, scribing to walls, and integrating with plumbing/electrical/countertops/flooring/appliances. Countertops are typically *fabricated + installed by a specialty fabricator* (stone/quartz) — a sub within the scope. So this manual covers both the **install-and-coordinate** reality (cabinets/built-ins, plausibly self-performed) and the **specify-and-check** reality (countertops, often a fabricator sub).

**⚠️ PRECISION / COORDINATION BOUNDARY:** Cabinetry is **finish work judged up close** — gaps, out-of-level runs, misaligned doors, and bad scribes show and look unprofessional. And it's a **coordination hub**: cabinets must integrate with plumbing (sink/faucet rough-in), electrical (outlets/under-cabinet/appliance circuits — cross-ref Electrical), countertops (the fabricator templates *after* cabinets are set), appliances (fit + connections), and flooring (sequence). Throughout, **⚠️** marks where *install precision* or *cross-trade coordination/sequencing* matters — these are where cabinetry jobs go wrong (a sink base not roughed for plumbing, a countertop templated off out-of-level cabinets, an appliance that doesn't fit the opening).

**How to use this manual (for the AI):**
1. Identify the scope (cabinets, countertops, vanities, built-ins) + who supplies what (stock/custom; fabricator).
2. Use **Required Dimensions** (a layout/cabinet list + countertop SF); estimate conservatively + state assumptions.
3. Build from **cabinet count + countertop SF + install labor + coordination items**.
4. Estimate labor from **Labor Tasks**, apply **Modifiers** (one combined factor, capped).
5. Add **Equipment**; apply **Best-Practice Notes** (incl. precision/coordination ⚠️).

**Units key:** cabinet/box = a single cabinet unit · LF = linear foot (a common cabinetry shorthand — "X linear feet of cabinets") · SF = square foot (countertops) · EA = each · MH = man-hours · ⚠️ = install precision / cross-trade coordination — verify fit + sequence

**Estimating note:** Cabinets are commonly counted **per box** or by **linear feet of run**, plus the **install labor** (set/level/scribe/trim). Countertops are **SF (fabricated + installed, usually by a fabricator)**. The cabinet *material* cost is supplier-driven (stock → semi-custom → custom is a wide range); the *contractor* scope is layout + install + coordination. Give ranges; cabinet/countertop material varies enormously by tier.

# 1. KITCHEN CABINETS (LAYOUT, SET & INSTALL)

## 1.1 System overview & types

Kitchen cabinets are the boxes (base, wall, tall/pantry) that define the kitchen — bought as stock, semi-custom, or custom, then **set level/plumb/square, scribed to walls, fastened together + to the wall, and trimmed.** The estimate is driven by **the cabinet count / linear feet (and tier — stock vs. custom drives material), the install complexity (a simple galley vs. a complex layout with islands/tall units/specialty), the wall/floor condition (out-of-level/plumb walls + floors mean scribing/shimming ⚠️), the coordination (plumbing/electrical/appliances ⚠️), and the trim/finish (toe kick, crown, fillers, panels).** The cabinets are usually a supplier cost; the contractor's value is the precise install + coordination.

**Cabinet tiers (drive material cost):**
- **Stock** — pre-made, standard sizes/finishes, fast/cheap, limited options (fill gaps with fillers). 
- **Semi-custom** — stock construction with more sizes/finishes/modifications — the common middle.
- **Custom** — built to spec (any size/material/finish) — premium, fits any layout, longest lead.
- **RTA (ready-to-assemble)** — flat-pack, assembled on site (cheaper, assembly labor ⚠️).

**Cabinet types (the boxes):** base (floor), wall (upper), tall/pantry/oven, specialty (corner/lazy-susan, sink base, drawer base, appliance garage, island). **Construction:** framed vs. frameless (full-access/Euro); quality (plywood vs. particleboard box, dovetail drawers, soft-close) affects cost + durability.

## 1.2 Required dimensions for an accurate quote

- **★ Cabinet list / layout** — count + sizes of each box (base/wall/tall/specialty), or **linear feet of run** + a layout. **The core takeoff.**
- **★ Tier** — stock / semi-custom / custom / RTA (material cost + lead time).
- **★ Layout complexity** — galley/L/U/island; tall units; specialty cabinets; number of corners.
- **★ Wall + floor condition** ⚠️ — out-of-plumb walls, out-of-level floors → scribing + shimming ⚠️.
- **★ Coordination** ⚠️ — sink base (plumbing rough-in ⚠️), appliance openings + connections (electrical/plumbing/gas ⚠️ — cross-ref Electrical/Plumbing/HVAC), outlets/under-cabinet lighting (electrical ⚠️).
- **★ Trim / finish components** — toe kick, crown molding, light rail, fillers, end/island panels, scribe molding.
- **★ Countertop** — (§2 — templated *after* cabinets ⚠️).
- **★ Appliances** — sizes + fit (the openings must match ⚠️); who supplies/installs.
- **Removal of old kitchen?** — demo (cross-ref — and coordinate plumbing/electrical disconnect).
- **Flooring sequence** ⚠️ — flooring before or after cabinets? (cross-ref Flooring).

## 1.7 Best-practice notes that change material or labor

1. **⚠️ Layout + leveling is the foundation — everything follows the level lines.** The install starts by finding the **high point of the floor + establishing level reference lines** ⚠️ — base cabinets are set to a level line (shimmed up from the high point), uppers to a level line. **Out-of-level cabinets cascade into countertop, appliance, and door problems** ⚠️. The layout/leveling setup is the make-or-break first step; rushing it ruins the job. The #1 cabinetry best-practice.
2. **⚠️ Walls + floors are never perfectly plumb/level — scribing is the skill.** Real walls bow/lean and floors slope ⚠️ — so cabinets need **scribing (cutting the cabinet/filler edge to follow the wall) + shimming** ⚠️ to fit gap-free and sit level. **Fillers + scribe molding close the gaps** stock cabinets leave. The scribing/shimming skill (and the fillers) is what separates a professional install from gaps + a crooked run. Budget for it on any real-world install.
3. **⚠️ Coordinate plumbing, electrical, and appliances — the coordination hub.** The sink base must align with the plumbing rough-in ⚠️ (cross-ref Plumbing); outlets, under-cabinet lighting, and appliance circuits with electrical ⚠️ (cross-ref Electrical); appliance openings must match the actual appliance sizes ⚠️ (and gas/water/electric connections — cross-ref HVAC/Plumbing/Electrical). **A sink base not roughed for plumbing, or an appliance that doesn't fit the opening, is a classic, costly cabinetry failure.** Verify all of it up front + sequence the trades. This coordination is much of the contractor's value.
4. **⚠️ Countertops are templated AFTER cabinets are set (and must be level).** The countertop fabricator (§2) **templates off the installed cabinets** ⚠️ — so the cabinets must be set, level, and final before templating ⚠️. Out-of-level cabinets = a bad countertop fit/gaps. Sequence: cabinets set + level → template → fabricate → install counter. Don't promise a countertop date before cabinets are set.
5. **⚠️ Door/drawer alignment + even reveals = the visible quality.** Adjusting doors/drawers so reveals (gaps) are even + consistent and everything aligns ⚠️ is the finish detail people see up close — misaligned doors look cheap regardless of cabinet quality. Soft-close adjustment too. A real (if small-per-unit) labor item that defines perceived quality.
6. **Cabinet tier drives material cost enormously.** Stock (cheap/fast/limited) → semi-custom (middle) → custom (premium/any-fit/long-lead) → RTA (cheap + assembly labor ⚠️). The cabinet *material* is the big cost and the supplier drives it; lead times vary a lot (custom = weeks/months — a schedule driver). Confirm the tier + lead time.
7. **Construction quality matters (plywood vs. particleboard, drawers, hinges).** Plywood boxes (vs. particleboard), dovetail drawers (vs. stapled), full-extension soft-close slides, and quality hinges affect durability + cost. A cheap box looks fine installed but fails sooner. A spec the customer should understand.
8. **Hardware = per-piece labor + consistency.** Pulls/knobs are per-door/drawer + must be consistently placed (a jig ⚠️) — many pieces add up, and inconsistent placement looks amateur. Soft-close/specialty hinges + slides too. Real (if light) labor.
9. **Demo + disconnect coordination (replace).** Removing an old kitchen means coordinating plumbing + electrical + gas disconnect ⚠️ (cross-ref Plumbing/Electrical/HVAC) before demo, then the new rough-in adjustments. A real coordination + scope item on a remodel (not just "rip out the old cabinets").
10. **⚠️ Flooring sequence decision.** Whether flooring goes *before* cabinets (flooring under cabinets — common, and needed for floating floors that shouldn't be pinned) or *after* (cabinets first, floor to them) ⚠️ — affects sequence + cost + future-flooring-replacement (cross-ref Flooring). A real sequencing decision to settle up front.
11. **Trim/crown is skilled finish carpentry.** Crown molding, light rails, decorative panels, and toe kick are finish carpentry (cross-ref Carpentry) — crown especially is skilled (miters/copes on out-of-square corners). Adds real labor on a trimmed-out kitchen.
12. **Confirm cabinet list, tier, coordination, sequence, level** ⚠️ — the right kitchen job specifies the cabinet list/layout + tier, the wall/floor condition (scribing ⚠️), the plumbing/electrical/appliance coordination ⚠️, the countertop-template sequence ⚠️, the flooring sequence ⚠️, and the trim — together. A cabinet quote without the coordination + level + sequence context is incomplete (and coordination failures are the costly ones).

## 1.8 Quick worked example

Kitchen remodel, ~20 LF of cabinets (mix of base/wall/tall) + island, stock+, replace (illustrative):
- **Cabinets:** the box list (base/wall/tall/sink base/island/specialty) — supplier cost (tier + lead time ⚠️); + fillers, toe kick, crown, island panels, hardware.
- **Demo** old kitchen + **coordinate plumbing/electrical/gas disconnect** ⚠️ (cross-ref Plumbing/Electrical/HVAC).
- **Layout + level** ⚠️ (find high point, level lines); **set + scribe + shim** base + wall + island ⚠️ (out-of-level floor/walls); **coordinate sink base (plumbing ⚠️), appliance openings + fit ⚠️, outlets/under-cabinet (electrical ⚠️)**.
- **Countertop** (§2) **templated after cabinets set + level** ⚠️ → fabricated → installed.
- **Adjust doors/drawers (even reveals) ⚠️ + hardware**.
- **Labor:** demo + layout/level ⚠️ + set/scribe ~ (boxes × ~1 MH + scribing) + island + trim/crown + hardware + door adjust + coordination ≈ a **skilled multi-day** install; layout/leveling + scribing + coordination are the craft.
- **Equipment:** laser level, drill/clamps/shims, scribe tools, stud finder, miter saw, cabinet jacks, hardware jig.
- *Key:* **level setup + scribing + plumbing/electrical/appliance coordination + countertop-template sequence** ⚠️ — the cabinets are bought; the *install + coordination* is the value (and where it goes wrong).

# 2. COUNTERTOPS

## 2.1 System overview & types

Countertops are the work surface — usually **fabricated + installed by a specialty fabricator** (especially stone/quartz: templated, cut, edged, and set by the fabricator as a sub within the scope), though some (laminate, butcher block) are more contractor-installable. The estimate is driven by **the material (a huge cost range — laminate to premium stone), the square footage + linear feet, the edge profile + cutouts (sink/cooktop), the fabrication complexity (seams, waterfall edges, backsplash), and the template-fabricate-install sequence** ⚠️ (templated *after* cabinets are set ⚠️). For stone/quartz, the fabricator is effectively a sub; the contractor coordinates + checks.

**Materials (drive cost + who installs):**
- **Quartz (engineered stone)** — popular, durable, non-porous (no sealing), consistent, wide colors — fabricator-installed. Premium-but-common.
- **Granite / natural stone (marble/quartzite/etc.)** ⚠️ — natural, premium, each slab unique; porous (seal ⚠️); fabricator-installed. Marble etches/stains (care ⚠️).
- **Laminate** — economical, wide patterns, **contractor-installable** (post-form or custom), not as durable/heat-resistant. The budget option.
- **Solid surface (e.g. Corian)** — seamless, repairable, mid-range — fabricator/certified installer.
- **Butcher block / wood** — warm, **contractor-installable**, needs sealing/maintenance ⚠️ (water/heat care).
- **Concrete** ⚠️ — custom, heavy, sealed (cross-ref Concrete) — specialty.
- **Tile** — tiled countertop (less common now) — (cross-ref Flooring/tile).
- **Stainless / specialty** — commercial/specialty.

## 2.2 Required dimensions for an accurate quote

- **★ Material** — laminate → solid surface → quartz → natural stone (huge cost range + who installs).
- **★ Square footage + linear feet** — the counter area + run; plus backsplash (if same material).
- **★ Edge profile** — eased/bullnose/ogee/mitered/waterfall (complexity + cost).
- **★ Cutouts** — sink (under/drop-in/farmhouse ⚠️), cooktop, faucet holes — each a fabrication item.
- **★ Sink type** ⚠️ — undermount (stone/quartz — fabricated cutout ⚠️) vs. drop-in; farmhouse (cabinet + counter coordination ⚠️).
- **★ Seams** — slab size vs. counter run → seam placement (large kitchens need seams ⚠️).
- **★ Backsplash** — material counter run / full-height; same-material vs. tile (cross-ref Flooring/tile).
- **★ Template sequence** ⚠️ — templated AFTER cabinets set + level ⚠️.
- **Overhangs / islands** — eating overhangs (support/corbels for big overhangs ⚠️).
- **Who fabricates/installs** — fabricator (stone/quartz) vs. contractor (laminate/butcher block).

| **Fabrication (cutting/edging/cutouts/seams)** | (in fabricator price) | Stone/quartz — in the fabricator's SF price. |
| **Edge profile** | LF | Eased (basic) → ogee/mitered (premium). |
| **Sink/cooktop cutouts** | EA | Each cutout. |

| **SF + LF** | Area + run. |

**Rough scoping (ranges — verify with the fabricator):** Countertops range from **economical (laminate, contractor-installed)** to **premium (natural stone, fabricator-installed)** — priced largely **per SF (material + fabrication + install)** with edge/cutout/seam adders. For stone/quartz, **the fabricator's quote (templated off set cabinets ⚠️) is the number** — the contractor coordinates + checks. Laminate/butcher block the contractor may self-install.

## 2.5 Best-practice / coordination notes (for reading the bid / coordinating)

1. **⚠️ Templated AFTER cabinets are set + level — the critical sequence.** Stone/quartz/solid-surface fabricators **template off the installed, leveled cabinets** ⚠️ — so cabinets must be fully set + level + final before templating ⚠️ (out-of-level cabinets = a bad-fitting counter with gaps). **Sequence: cabinets set + level → template → fabricate (days/weeks) → install** ⚠️. Build the fabrication lead time into the schedule, and don't template off unfinished cabinets. The #1 countertop coordination rule.
2. **Material is the dominant cost — and varies enormously.** Laminate (economical, contractor-installed) → solid surface → quartz (popular, durable, no-seal) → natural stone (premium, unique, seal ⚠️). The material choice is the biggest cost lever; present the range. Stone/quartz = fabricator-installed (a sub); laminate/butcher block = contractor-installable.
3. **⚠️ Undermount + farmhouse sinks add fabrication + coordination.** Undermount sinks (common with stone/quartz) need a fabricated, polished cutout + mounting ⚠️; farmhouse/apron sinks need cabinet + countertop coordination ⚠️ (a special sink base + the counter detail). Drop-in sinks are simpler. The sink type drives fabrication + plumbing/cabinet coordination (cross-ref Plumbing/§1). Confirm it early.
4. **⚠️ Seams — placement + visibility.** Slabs have size limits, so large kitchens/islands need seams ⚠️ — **placed thoughtfully** (not in the most visible spot, often at a sink/cooktop) and well-joined. More seams = more fabrication + visible joints. Slab size vs. layout drives the seam count. A quality fabricator plans seams well.
5. **Edge profile + cutouts are fabrication adders.** Premium edges (ogee, mitered/thick, waterfall) and each cutout (sink/cooktop/faucet) add fabrication cost. Basic eased edge is the baseline. Confirm the edge + count the cutouts.
6. **⚠️ Big overhangs need support.** Eating overhangs (islands/peninsulas) beyond ~10–12" need support (corbels/brackets/steel) ⚠️ — unsupported overhangs crack (stone) or sag. A structural detail on islands. Plan support for big overhangs.
7. **⚠️ Natural stone needs sealing + care.** Granite/marble/quartzite are porous — seal them ⚠️ (and marble etches/stains — customer care ⚠️). Quartz doesn't (non-porous). A maintenance reality to set with the customer (and a fabrication step for stone).
8. **Backsplash coordination.** Backsplash can be the same material (continued up — fabricated) or tile (cross-ref Flooring/tile) — a separate material/labor decision, and tile backsplash coordinates with the counter + outlets (electrical ⚠️). Confirm the backsplash scope.
9. **Contractor-installable materials (laminate/butcher block).** Laminate (post-form or custom) and butcher block are often contractor-installed (vs. fabricator) — a self-perform option for budget/specific looks (butcher block needs sealing/maintenance ⚠️). Different scope than a stone fabricator.
10. **Coordinate with plumbing + cooktop after install.** The sink/faucet (plumbing trim — cross-ref Plumbing) and cooktop connect *after* the counter is installed — sequence the plumber/appliance after the counter. A final-coordination point.

## 2.6 Quick worked example

Kitchen quartz countertops, ~50 SF + island, undermount sink (illustrative):
- **Material:** ~50 SF quartz (fabricator-supplied + installed — a sub); **edge profile** (e.g. eased/ogee); **undermount sink cutout** ⚠️ + faucet holes + cooktop cutout; **seam** placement (island/run ⚠️); **island overhang support** if big ⚠️.
- **Sequence** ⚠️: cabinets set + level (§1) → **fabricator templates** ⚠️ → fabricates (days–weeks) → installs.
- **Coordinate:** sink base (plumbing ⚠️ — §1/Plumbing), then **plumber sets sink/faucet after counter** ⚠️; backsplash (same-material or tile) decision.
- **Cost:** the **fabricator's per-SF quote** (material + fabrication + cutouts + edge + install) is the number; contractor coordinates + checks.
- *Key:* **template only after cabinets are set + level** ⚠️, plan the **seam + sink cutout + overhang support**, and **sequence plumbing after the counter** — the fabricator does the work; coordination + sequence is the contractor's job.

# 3. BATH VANITIES & CABINETS

## 3.1 System overview & types

Bath cabinetry — vanities (sink base), medicine cabinets, linen/storage, and the vanity top. Smaller-scale than a kitchen but **moisture-exposed ⚠️ and tightly coordinated with plumbing** ⚠️ (the vanity houses the sink + plumbing). The estimate is driven by **the vanity (size/tier — stock to custom), the vanity top (material — often a smaller stone/quartz/cultured-marble fabricated piece), the install (set/level/scribe + plumbing coordination ⚠️), and moisture-appropriate materials/finish** ⚠️.

**Types:**
- **Vanity (sink base)** — the main piece; freestanding, floating (wall-hung — wall blocking needed ⚠️), or furniture-style. Single/double sink. Stock → custom.
- **Vanity top** — cultured marble (integral sink, economical), quartz/granite (fabricated, undermount/drop-in sink), or a separate top + vessel sink. Often a smaller fabricated piece.
- **Medicine cabinet / mirror** — recessed (wall coordination ⚠️) or surface.
- **Linen / storage cabinets** — tall/wall storage.
- **Moisture consideration** ⚠️ — baths are humid/wet; materials + finishes should tolerate moisture ⚠️ (and ventilation matters — cross-ref HVAC).

## 3.2 Required dimensions for an accurate quote

- **★ Vanity size/type + tier** — width, single/double, freestanding/floating ⚠️ (blocking), stock/custom.
- **★ Vanity top material + sink** — cultured marble (integral) / quartz/granite (fabricated + sink cutout ⚠️) / vessel.
- **★ Plumbing coordination** ⚠️ — sink/faucet rough-in alignment ⚠️ (cross-ref Plumbing).
- **★ Floating vanity?** ⚠️ — wall blocking/structure to hang it ⚠️ (cross-ref Framing).

## 3.7 Best-practice notes that change material or labor

1. **⚠️ Coordinate with plumbing — the vanity houses the plumbing.** The vanity must align with the sink/faucet rough-in ⚠️ (cross-ref Plumbing), and the plumber sets the faucet/sink trim *after* the vanity + top are in ⚠️. A vanity set without matching the rough-in, or a sequence that traps the plumbing, is a classic bath-cabinetry problem. Verify rough-in alignment + sequence the plumber after. (Same coordination logic as the kitchen sink base.)
2. **⚠️ Moisture-appropriate materials + finishes (baths are wet).** Bath cabinetry lives in humidity/splash ⚠️ — use moisture-tolerant materials/finishes (and ensure bath ventilation works — cross-ref HVAC; a poorly ventilated bath ruins cabinetry). Particleboard with a poor finish swells in a wet bath. A material consideration the kitchen doesn't stress as much.
3. **⚠️ Floating vanities need wall blocking/structure.** Wall-hung (floating) vanities must hang on adequate blocking/structure in the wall ⚠️ (cross-ref Framing) — added during framing/before drywall, or retrofitted. A floating vanity on inadequate blocking pulls out. Plan the blocking up front if floating.
4. **⚠️ Recessed medicine cabinets need wall coordination.** A recessed medicine cabinet requires cutting/framing the wall cavity ⚠️ (between studs, avoiding plumbing/electrical/wires) — more work than a surface-mount. Plan it (and watch for what's in the wall ⚠️).
5. **Vanity top options + sink.** Cultured marble (integral sink, economical, one-piece), fabricated stone/quartz (undermount/drop-in sink, premium — a smaller fabricated piece ⚠️), or a separate top + vessel sink. The top + sink choice drives cost + plumbing coordination. 
6. **Scribe + level (as kitchens).** Vanities still need leveling + scribing to imperfect walls/floors ⚠️ (same skill as §1) — a single vanity is small but should still be level + gap-free.
7. **Demo + disconnect (replace).** Replacing a vanity = plumbing disconnect ⚠️ (cross-ref Plumbing) + demo, then reset. Coordinate the plumber.
8. **Electrical coordination (lighting/outlets).** Vanity lighting + outlets (cross-ref Electrical — and code GFCI near water ⚠️) coordinate with the vanity/mirror. A real coordination point.
9. **Confirm vanity, top, plumbing coordination, blocking, moisture** ⚠️ — the right bath-cabinetry job specifies the vanity (tier, floating? ⚠️), the top + sink, the plumbing coordination ⚠️, any blocking (floating) ⚠️ or recessed-cabinet wall work ⚠️, and moisture-appropriate materials ⚠️ — together. Plumbing coordination is the main thing.

## 3.8 Quick worked example

Bath vanity replacement, 48" double-sink, quartz top, freestanding (illustrative):
- **Vanity:** 48" double (tier-driven) + **quartz top + undermount sinks** (small fabricated piece + cutouts ⚠️); moisture-appropriate ⚠️.
- **Demo** old vanity + **plumbing disconnect** ⚠️; verify **rough-in aligns** with the new double-sink layout ⚠️ (cross-ref Plumbing).
- **Set + level + scribe** ⚠️; set top; **plumber sets faucets/sinks after** ⚠️; coordinate vanity lighting/outlets (electrical — GFCI ⚠️).
- **Labor:** demo + disconnect ⚠️ + set/level/scribe (~1–2 MH) + top + hardware + plumbing-trim coordination ⚠️ ≈ a **quick skilled** install; the plumbing coordination ⚠️ is the focus.
- *Key:* **align with the plumbing rough-in + sequence the plumber after + moisture-appropriate materials** ⚠️ — a small job that lives or dies on plumbing coordination.

# 4. BUILT-INS & SPECIALTY MILLWORK

## 4.1 System overview & types

Built-ins and specialty cabinetry — custom storage + furniture built into the space: bookcases, entertainment centers, window seats, mudroom lockers/benches, closet systems, home-office built-ins, fireplace surrounds, wet bars, and architectural millwork. More **custom + carpentry-intensive** than modular cabinets (often built/assembled on site or shop-built then fitted), and judged as fine finish work ⚠️. The estimate is driven by **the scope/complexity (a simple bookcase vs. a wall of furniture-grade built-ins), custom vs. modular-based, the material/finish (paint-grade vs. stain-grade hardwood ⚠️), the site-fitting (scribing to the space ⚠️), and any coordination (electrical for entertainment/office, plumbing for a wet bar ⚠️).** This overlaps finish carpentry (cross-ref Carpentry).

**Types:**
- **Bookcases / shelving** — built-in shelving (adjustable/fixed); simple → architectural.
- **Entertainment / media centers** — TV/media built-ins (electrical + ventilation + cable coordination ⚠️).
- **Window seats / benches / mudroom lockers** — storage seating; mudroom cubbies/benches/hooks (common, practical).
- **Closet systems** — organizers (shelving/rods/drawers) — modular or custom.
- **Home-office built-ins** — desks/storage (electrical coordination ⚠️).
- **Fireplace surrounds / mantels** — millwork around a fireplace (clearances ⚠️ — cross-ref HVAC/code).
- **Wet bars** — cabinetry + sink/plumbing ⚠️ + sometimes electrical (cross-ref Plumbing/Electrical).
- **Architectural millwork** — wainscot, coffered/beam ceilings, paneling (cross-ref Carpentry) — fine finish.
- **Approach:** can start from stock/modular cabinets (as a base) + custom trim/tops, or be fully custom-built (shop or on-site).

## 4.2 Required dimensions for an accurate quote

- **★ Scope + dimensions** — what's being built, size, complexity (a measured design/drawing).
- **★ Custom vs. modular-based** — fully custom-built vs. stock-cabinet base + custom finish.
- **★ Material + finish** ⚠️ — paint-grade (MDF/poplar) vs. stain-grade hardwood ⚠️ (cost + finish labor); finished on site or shop.
- **★ Site-fitting** ⚠️ — scribing/fitting to the existing space (walls/floors/ceilings out of square ⚠️).
- **★ Coordination** ⚠️ — electrical (media/office/lighting — cross-ref Electrical), plumbing (wet bar — cross-ref Plumbing), fireplace clearances ⚠️.
- **★ Complexity of joinery/detail** — simple shelving vs. furniture-grade (face frames, doors/drawers, moldings, glass).
- **Finish** — who finishes (paint/stain — cross-ref Interior Finish), site or shop.
- **Shop-built vs. on-site** — affects labor location + fitting.

## 4.7 Best-practice notes that change material or labor

1. **Complexity is the dominant driver — estimate the specific project.** Built-ins span a simple bookcase (a day) to furniture-grade walls of cabinetry or coffered ceilings (major custom work) — there's no single unit rate. **Estimate by the actual scope, joinery, and detail.** The biggest variable is furniture-grade detail (face frames, doors/drawers, moldings, glass) vs. simple shelving.
2. **⚠️ Scribing/fitting to the space is the craft.** Built-ins fit into existing spaces with out-of-square walls/floors/ceilings ⚠️ — **scribing + fitting** for a seamless, built-in look (vs. obvious gaps) is the finish skill (cross-ref Carpentry). The "built-in" quality is in the fit. Budget for fitting.
3. **⚠️ Paint-grade vs. stain-grade changes material + finish.** Paint-grade (MDF/poplar — hides joints, cheaper) vs. stain-grade hardwood (shows the wood + every defect ⚠️, pricier material, more finish care) ⚠️. Stain-grade demands better material + workmanship (defects show through stain). A cost + quality decision (cross-ref Interior Finish for the actual finishing).
4. **⚠️ Coordinate electrical/plumbing/fireplace.** Media centers (outlets/cable/ventilation for electronics ⚠️), home offices (outlets/data ⚠️), wet bars (plumbing + electrical ⚠️ — cross-ref Plumbing/Electrical), and fireplace surrounds (combustible clearances ⚠️ — cross-ref HVAC/code) need coordination. A built-in with electronics/plumbing/fire isn't just cabinetry. Plan the coordination.
5. **Modular base vs. fully custom.** Using stock/modular cabinets as a base (+ custom trim/tops/panels to integrate) is faster/cheaper than fully custom-built — a smart approach for many built-ins (e.g. an entertainment center on stock bases). Fully custom fits anything but costs more. Confirm the approach.
6. **Shop-built vs. on-site.** Shop-built (then fitted on site) gives better-controlled quality + finish; on-site-built fits the space directly. Each has tradeoffs (shop = quality/finish; on-site = fit/transport). Affects where the labor happens.
7. **Mudroom/practical built-ins are common + valuable.** Mudroom lockers/benches/cubbies (with hooks/storage) and closet systems are practical, popular, and a good built-in niche (especially here — snowy mudroom abuse). Durable, functional scopes.
8. **Finish coordination (paint/stain).** Built-ins are painted/stained (site or shop — cross-ref Interior Finish) — coordinate the finish (and sequence: prime/paint before or after install? site-finish needs protection of the room). A real finish-trade coordination.
9. **⚠️ Fireplace surround clearances.** Millwork around a fireplace must respect combustible clearances ⚠️ (cross-ref HVAC/code) — non-combustible materials/clearances near the firebox. A code/safety detail on fireplace built-ins.
10. **Confirm scope, custom level, material/finish, coordination, fitting** ⚠️ — the right built-in job specifies the scope + complexity, custom vs. modular-based, paint vs. stain-grade ⚠️, the electrical/plumbing/fireplace coordination ⚠️, the site-fitting/scribing ⚠️, and the finish — together. Estimate the specific project; the fit + detail + coordination are the craft.

Living-room built-in entertainment center + flanking bookcases, paint-grade, ~12 LF wall (illustrative):
- **Approach:** stock cabinet bases (lowers) + custom uppers/shelving/trim, OR fully custom; **paint-grade** (MDF/poplar) ⚠️.
- **Coordinate** ⚠️: media outlets + cable + ventilation for electronics ⚠️ (cross-ref Electrical), TV mounting/structure.
- **Build/assemble** (shop or on-site) → **install + scribe/fit** to the wall/floor/ceiling ⚠️ (out-of-square) → trim/molding (cross-ref Carpentry) → doors/shelves + hardware (even reveals ⚠️).
- **Finish:** paint (site or shop — cross-ref Interior Finish) + protect the room.
- **Labor:** design/measure ⚠️ + build (complexity-driven) + install/scribe/fit ⚠️ + trim + hardware + electrical coordination ⚠️ + finish ≈ a **skilled custom** project — estimate by the specific scope; fit + detail + media coordination are the craft.
- *Key:* **estimate the specific scope** (no unit rate), nail the **scribing/fit + media coordination + paint-grade finish** ⚠️ — built-ins are custom finish carpentry, not modular cabinets.

# APPENDIX — Cross-system cabinetry principles

1. **Cabinetry estimates by the box + countertop SF + install precision + coordination — not just area.** Cabinets are counted per box / linear feet (material supplier-driven by tier ⚠️); countertops by SF (usually fabricator-installed); the **contractor's value is layout, precise install, scribing, and coordination** ⚠️. Built-ins are custom (estimate by project). Give ranges — material tiers (stock → custom; laminate → stone) vary enormously.
2. **⚠️ Layout + leveling is the foundation.** Everything follows level reference lines from the floor's high point ⚠️ — out-of-level cabinets cascade into countertop, appliance, and door problems. The leveling setup is the make-or-break first step.
3. **⚠️ Scribing to imperfect walls/floors is the install skill.** Real walls/floors are never plumb/level ⚠️ — scribing + shimming + fillers for a gap-free, level result is what separates professional from amateur. Budget for it on every real-world install (kitchens, vanities, built-ins).
4. **⚠️ Cabinetry is a coordination hub — plumbing, electrical, appliances, countertops, flooring.** Sink bases ↔ plumbing rough-in ⚠️; outlets/under-cabinet/appliance circuits ↔ electrical ⚠️ (cross-ref Electrical); appliance openings ↔ actual appliance sizes + connections ⚠️ (cross-ref Plumbing/HVAC); **countertops templated AFTER cabinets are set + level** ⚠️; flooring sequence (under or to cabinets) ⚠️ (cross-ref Flooring). **Coordination failures (a sink base not roughed, an appliance that won't fit, a counter templated off out-of-level cabinets) are the costly cabinetry mistakes** — verify + sequence up front. This coordination is much of the contractor's value.
5. **⚠️ The countertop template-fabricate-install sequence drives the schedule.** Stone/quartz is templated off set + level cabinets ⚠️, then fabricated (days–weeks), then installed — build the lead time in, and don't template off unfinished cabinets. Plumbing/cooktop connect *after* the counter. A key sequencing rule.
6. **Material tier is the dominant material cost — and a lead-time driver.** Stock → semi-custom → custom cabinets (and laminate → quartz → stone counters) is a wide cost range, and custom = long lead times ⚠️ (a schedule driver). Confirm tier + lead time; the cabinet/counter material is usually supplier/fabricator-driven (the contractor coordinates + installs).
7. **One combined difficulty factor, capped (~1.4×), on install labor.** Out-of-square space (scribing), complex layout, specialty/custom, coordination-heavy — pick the governing one. Demo + disconnect ⚠️, RTA assembly, crown/trim, hardware, countertop fabrication, blocking (floating) ⚠️, and finishing are **separate line items**.
8. **Door/drawer alignment + even reveals = perceived quality.** The visible up-close quality is in even reveals + aligned doors/drawers ⚠️ + consistent hardware — a real (light-per-unit) labor item that defines whether the job looks professional regardless of cabinet cost.
9. **Built-ins overlap finish carpentry — estimate by the specific project.** Custom built-ins/millwork (cross-ref Carpentry) have no unit rate — the complexity, joinery, fit, finish, and coordination drive them. Paint-grade vs. stain-grade ⚠️ changes material + finish.
10. **Cross-references:** **sink-base/vanity plumbing rough-in + wet bar + faucet trim-after** ↔ Plumbing Manual ⚠️; **outlets/under-cabinet/appliance circuits + GFCI + media/office** ↔ Electrical Manual ⚠️; **appliance fit + connections + gas + fireplace clearances** ↔ HVAC/Plumbing/Electrical ⚠️; **flooring-vs-cabinet sequence + countertop edge/tile backsplash** ↔ Flooring Manual ⚠️; **floating-vanity/built-in blocking + structure** ↔ Framing Manual ⚠️; **crown/trim/built-in millwork + paint/stain finishing** ↔ Carpentry + Interior Finish manuals. Confirm the *whole* scope (cabinets + counters + install + coordination + sequence), not just the boxes.
