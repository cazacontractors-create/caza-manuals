# Caza Contractors — Siding Estimating Manual

**Purpose:** A trade-expert reference for the AI estimator covering exterior wall cladding — vinyl, metal/steel, fiber cement, wood/engineered wood — plus the water-management layer (WRB/flashing) and trim that every siding job depends on. Same structure as the roofing and framing manuals: for each siding system it defines the labor tasks that drive time, the factors that move them, the material list with units and coverage rates, special equipment, the dimensions required for an accurate quote, and the best-practices that change material or labor.

**Siding is "surface" estimating (like roofing):** wall area drives the field cladding; the perimeter, openings, and transitions drive the trim and flashing — which is where the labor and the leaks concentrate. The water-resistive barrier (WRB) and flashing behind/around the siding are not optional and not an afterthought; on a re-side they're often the most important part of the job.

**How to use this manual (for the AI):**
1. Identify the siding system from the work type/description.
2. Use **Required Dimensions** to confirm inputs; estimate conservatively + state assumptions if missing.
3. Build field cladding from **wall area ÷ coverage × (1 + waste)**; build trim/flashing from **opening count + perimeter/transition LF**.
4. Estimate labor from **Labor Tasks** (MH/unit), then apply **Modifiers** (one combined factor, capped).
5. Add **Equipment** for the scope.
6. Apply **Best-Practice Notes** — they change material or labor.

**Units key:** SQ = square (100 sq ft of wall) · SF = square foot · LF = linear foot · EA = each · MH = man-hours · WRB = water-resistive barrier · OC = on-center

**Wall-area basis:** Siding area = gross wall area (wall length × height, all elevations, including gables) **minus large openings** (subtract big windows/doors/garage doors; small windows often left in for waste). Gables add triangular area (½ × base × height). Always include gables, dormers, and any returns.

# 1. VINYL SIDING

## 1.1 System overview & sub-types

Vinyl is the economical, low-maintenance, fastest-to-install cladding: interlocking PVC panels hung on the wall with a deliberately **loose nailing** that lets them expand/contract (vinyl moves a lot with temperature — this drives the #1 install best-practice). The estimate is driven by **wall area (squares of field panel), the linear feet of trim/channel (J-channel, corners, starter — a big part of vinyl), the openings (each wrapped in channel), and the WRB behind it.**

**Sub-types / profiles (affect coverage and look, not the basic method):**
- **Horizontal lap (clapboard) — most common:** Double-4" (two 4" courses per panel), double-5", double-4.5" Dutch lap, triple-3". Exposure per panel ~8–10". The default.
- **Vertical (board & batten):** Vertical panels with a battened look; needs furring/different starter and trim; a bit more layout.
- **Shake/shingle vinyl:** Panels mimicking cedar shakes/scallops — gables, accents; higher cost, more per-square, slower.
- **Insulated vinyl:** Vinyl with a contoured foam backer (EPS) bonded behind — adds R-value, rigidity, flatter look, less oil-canning; heavier, pricier, slightly different install/trim depth.
- **Soffit (vented/solid) — related:** Vinyl soffit ties to the gutters/soffit/fascia scope; covered there, but often part of a vinyl job.

**Grade/thickness:** Builder-grade (~.040"), standard (~.044"), premium/thick (.046–.052"+) — thicker resists oil-canning and impact, higher cost, same method. Color: lighter colors hide expansion better; very dark colors absorb heat and move/warp more (a real consideration in sun).

## 1.2 Required dimensions for an accurate quote

- **★ Total wall area (SF or SQ)** — field panel. By elevation, including gables.
- **★ Gable areas** — triangular; ½ × base × height each. Often forgotten.
- **★ Number/size of openings (windows, doors, garage)** — each gets J-channel/trim wrap; subtract large ones from field area.
- **★ Outside corner count + height (LF)** — outside corner posts.
- **★ Inside corner count + height (LF)** — inside corner trim.
- **★ Starter strip length (LF)** — bottom of each sided wall section.
- **★ J-channel / finish LF** — around openings, at tops, at transitions (rooflines, soffits).
- **★ Wall height / number of stories** — staging/access, panel handling.
- **★ Re-side or new** — tear-off existing siding + WRB inspection/replacement, or over WRB on new sheathing.
- **Trim transitions** — to roof (against rake/eave), to other materials (stone/brick), at decks/porches.
- **Penetrations** — hose bibs, outlets, lights, vents, meters (each cut/trimmed/sealed → mounting blocks).
- **Soffit/fascia in scope?** — usually paired (see exterior-metal manual).
- **Substrate** — sheathing type (nailable?), existing siding condition, flatness.

## 1.7 Best-practice notes that change material or labor

1. **Vinyl MUST be hung loose to move — nail in the center of the slot, don't drive tight.** Vinyl expands/contracts significantly with temperature; nailed tight it buckles (waves) in heat and cracks in cold. Nails centered in slots, ~1/32" gap under the head, panels free to slide, and butt-overlap gaps left for movement (gap more in cold weather, less in heat). This is THE vinyl best-practice — getting it wrong makes a wavy/buckled wall. It's a workmanship point, not a material one, but it defines a good job.
2. **WRB behind siding is required — and on a re-side, inspect/replace it.** The water-resistive barrier (housewrap) is the actual water control; vinyl is not watertight (it's designed to drain/breathe behind). On a re-side, the old WRB (or felt, or nothing) is usually replaced — this is often the most valuable part of the job and a real line. Taped seams, lapped shingle-style, integrated with flashings.
3. **Flashing at openings and transitions is the leak defense.** Head flashing over windows/doors, kick-out flashing at roof-wall intersections (critical — missing kick-outs rot walls), proper integration with WRB (WRB over flashing flanges shingle-style). Vinyl trim/J-channel is NOT flashing — water gets behind vinyl by design, so the flashing/WRB underneath must manage it. Don't rely on caulk or J-channel to keep water out.
4. **Level the starter — errors compound.** The starter strip and first course set the level for the entire wall; if the starter isn't dead level, every course above amplifies the error and it shows badly at the top. Layout/leveling time up front is real and worth it.
5. **Don't caulk what must move.** Caulking vinyl butt joints, panel-to-channel, or other moving joints traps the panel and causes buckling/cracking. Caulk only at fixed penetrations/fixtures and where specified. A common DIY mistake that ruins the movement design.
6. **Gables, dormers, and returns add area and cut waste — include them.** Triangular gable area (½ base × height), dormer cheeks, and wall returns are easy to omit and add both field area and angle-cut waste. Always measure and include them.
7. **Mounting blocks for every penetration.** Lights, hose bibs, outlets, dryer/bath vents, meters — each needs a mounting block for a flat, sealed, finished surface (and to flash/seal properly). Cutting panels tight around a fixture without a block looks bad and leaks. A real per-penetration material + labor item.
8. **Substrate must be flat and nailable.** Vinyl telegraphs an uneven wall (waviness) and needs a nailable substrate (sheathing). Over uneven/old surfaces or non-nailable substrate, furring or a flat backer is required — a material + labor add. Insulated vinyl or foam backer helps flatness/rigidity.
9. **Cold-weather handling — vinyl gets brittle.** In cold (common here), vinyl cracks when cut/handled roughly and butt gaps must be larger (it's contracted). Score-and-snap or use the right blade; handle gently; account for seasonal expansion gaps. A workmanship/seasonal note that prevents cracked panels and buckling.
10. **Dark colors move and warp more.** Very dark vinyl absorbs heat, expands more, and can warp/distort (and may have heat-distortion warranty limits, especially near reflective windows or dark walls in sun). If dark colors are chosen, note the heat consideration; premium/thicker or heat-stable formulations help.
11. **J-channel quantity is a real driver on cut-up homes.** A house with many windows/doors and rooflines uses a lot of J-channel (sum every opening perimeter + every transition run). On trim-heavy elevations, channel + corners can rival the field labor. Estimate channel by actual LF, not a flat guess.
12. **Re-side: budget rot/sheathing repair as an allowance.** You won't see rot until the old siding's off — bottoms of walls, around windows, and roof-wall intersections are the usual spots. Budget a repair allowance and communicate it as an allowance, not a fixed number.

## 1.8 Quick worked example

Vinyl re-side, two-story house (illustrative):
- **Walls:** ~22 SQ field area (incl. gables), two-story, 14 windows + 2 doors, 4 outside corners (20 ft), 2 inside corners, re-side (tear off old vinyl, replace WRB).
- **Panels:** 22 SQ + ~12% ≈ **~25 SQ** (≈ 50 boxes).
- **Starter:** bottom-of-wall LF; **outside corners** 4 × ~20 ft ≈ 80 LF (8 posts of 10'); **inside corners** as needed.
- **J-channel:** sum of 16 opening perimeters + roofline/transition runs ≈ a large LF figure (this is a real line).
- **Mounting blocks:** per penetration (lights, bibs, outlets, vents, meter).
- **WRB:** ~22 SQ + lap (replaced); **flashing** at 16 openings + kick-outs at roof-walls.
- **Labor:** 25 SQ × ~2 MH/SQ × ~1.25 (two-story + cut-up) ≈ ~62 MH field + WRB (~10) + corners/starter/channel + 16 openings × ~0.7 (~11) + tear-off (22 × 0.6 ≈ 13) + flashing/blocks ≈ **~110–130 MH** + staging.
- **Equipment:** snap-lock/zip tools, snips, pump jacks/staging (two-story), brake if wrapping, dumpster.

# 2. METAL / STEEL SIDING

## 2.1 System overview & sub-types

Metal siding spans two pretty different worlds: **(A) agricultural/commercial exposed-fastener steel panels** (the same family as exposed-fastener roofing — Caza's barn-siding work) and **(B) residential metal siding** (steel/aluminum lap, board-&-batten, or panel systems, including modern concealed-fastener "standing-seam-look" wall panels). The estimate is driven by **wall area (panels by coverage width), the fasteners (real count on exposed-fastener), the trim package (corners, J-trim, base, transitions), and the WRB/flashing behind.**

**Sub-types:**
- **A) Ag/commercial exposed-fastener steel panel (R-panel/AG-rib, corrugated):** Ribbed/corrugated steel, ~36" net coverage, screwed through the face with gasketed screws, into girts/furring or sheathing. Trim: corner, base, J, eave/rake transitions, closures. Caza priced a steel-siding barn. Fast on big open walls. 26/29 ga, painted (SMP/Kynar) or Galvalume.
- **B) Residential steel/aluminum lap siding:** Horizontal lap profiles (mimicking wood lap), often concealed-nail hem, painted steel or aluminum. More trim/detail than ag panel; residential look. Insulated versions exist.
- **C) Concealed-fastener metal wall panel ("standing-seam-look" / flush / channel):** Architectural flush or reveal panels, concealed clips/fasteners — modern commercial/residential accent. More detailing, premium.
- **D) Metal board-&-batten / vertical panel:** Vertical metal, ag or residential; furring + vertical trim.

**Gauge/finish/material:** Steel (stronger, heavier, can rust if cut/scratched and unprotected — Galvalume or painted), aluminum (won't rust, softer/dents, good coastal), painted SMP (budget) vs. Kynar/PVDF (premium color retention). Affects cost, not coverage.

## 2.2 Required dimensions for an accurate quote

- **★ Wall area (SF/SQ)** by elevation incl. gables.
- **★ Panel coverage width** (ag ~36"; residential lap by exposure) — panel quantity = area ÷ coverage.
- **★ Panel run length / wall height** — panels cut to height; handling.
- **★ Openings count/size** — trim/flashing around each.
- **★ Outside/inside corner count + height (LF)** — corner trim.
- **★ Base/starter LF, eave/top transition LF, rake LF** — trim package.
- **★ Substrate: sheathing, girts/furring, or open framing** — fastener type/length, WRB approach.
- **Penetrations** — flashed/trimmed.
- **Re-side or new** — tear-off + WRB.
- **Closures (corrugated/ribbed profiles)** — foam closures at top/base/transitions.

## 2.7 Best-practice notes that change material or labor

1. **Cut metal cold — never abrasive cutoff wheels.** Abrasive grinding throws hot steel swarf that embeds and rusts (rust freckles), and burns the finish. Use shears, nibblers, or cold-cut blades. Touch up cut edges (especially steel) to prevent edge rust. Finish-warranty and appearance point.
2. **Drive exposed fasteners to correct depth — washer seal.** Like exposed-fastener roofing: over-driven crushes the neoprene washer, under-driven doesn't seal. Depth-control guns + skill. The wear/leak point of exposed-fastener systems.
3. **WRB and condensation control behind metal.** Metal isn't watertight (drains/breathes behind) and can condense on the back (warm moist interior air hitting cold metal). WRB required; over open framing/girts, condensation control (backed panel or membrane) prevents interior moisture problems. Especially relevant on ag/uninsulated walls.
4. **Fastener length/type for substrate.** Sheathing vs. steel girts vs. wood furring need different screws (self-drilling metal vs. wood, length to penetrate properly). Wrong fastener pulls out. Confirm substrate.
5. **Closures at top/base/transitions (ribbed/corrugated).** Foam closures fill corrugation gaps to block wind-driven rain, snow, insects, birds. Profile-specific. Real line on ag/ribbed.
6. **Flashing/trim integration with WRB at openings.** Head flashing over openings, base flashing/Z, transitions — integrated shingle-style with WRB so water that gets behind drains out. Trim is not the waterproofing; the WRB/flashing is.
7. **Thermal movement on long panels.** Metal expands/contracts; long wall panels need allowance (slotted holes/gasketed screws accommodate some). Long runs: per manufacturer.
8. **Dissimilar-metal and PT contact.** Avoid bare steel/Galvalume in contact with PT lumber or dissimilar metals (galvanic/chemical corrosion) — use separation/proper fasteners. Aluminum near masonry/PT similar caution.
9. **Steel scratches/edges = rust risk.** Handle to avoid scratching the finish; seal/touch-up cut edges. A scratched-to-bare-steel panel rusts. Appearance + longevity.
10. **Substrate flatness.** Ribbed panels hide some unevenness, but residential lap/flush panels telegraph a wavy wall — furring/flat substrate where needed.
11. **Gables/returns/dormers** — include the area and angle-cut waste (as vinyl).
12. **Snow/ice off adjacent metal roofs** — if metal siding is below a metal roof that sheds snow, detail the base/transition to take it (and consider snow retention on the roof) — a market detail.

## 2.8 Quick worked example

Steel-siding barn, exposed-fastener (Caza barn-job family, illustrative — ties to the ~$39,500 barn scope):
- **Walls:** large open area (e.g. ~24 SQ), single-story-tall barn walls, few openings, over girts/open framing, 29-ga painted ag panel (36" coverage).
- **Panels:** area ÷ 36" coverage, cut to wall height; ~24 SQ + ~7%.
- **Screws:** ag pattern × area → a real count (thousands); color-matched, length for girts.
- **Trim:** outside corners (count × height), base trim, J/opening trim around any doors/windows, eave/rake transition; **closures** at top/base.
- **WRB / condensation control** over framing; **flashing** at openings.
- **Labor:** 24 SQ × ~2 MH/SQ × ~1.0 (big open walls) ≈ ~48 MH field + trim/closures + openings + WRB ≈ **~70–90 MH** — fast, like ag roofing.
- **Equipment:** screw guns (depth control), metal shears/nibbler, brake, panel handling, magnetic sweeper, staging for tall walls.

# 3. FIBER CEMENT SIDING (e.g. HARDIE)

## 3.1 System overview & sub-types

Fiber cement (James Hardie being the dominant brand) is the premium, durable, fire-resistant, real-wood-look cladding: cement + sand + cellulose composite, very stable, long-lasting, paint-holding — but **heavy, hard on blades, dusty to cut (silica — a safety issue), and more labor-intensive than vinyl**. The estimate is driven by **wall area (squares of plank/panel), the trim (fiber cement or composite trim boards — substantial), the openings, fasteners (specific), and WRB/flashing.** Comes primed or factory-finished (ColorPlus); field-painted vs. pre-finished changes the finish scope.

**Sub-types:**
- **Lap siding (plank) — most common:** Horizontal planks (e.g. 8.25" with ~7" exposure), blind-nailed or face-nailed, on WRB over sheathing. The default. (HardiePlank etc.)
- **Panel (vertical sheet):** 4×8/4×10 panels (e.g. HardiePanel), often with batten strips for board-&-batten; faster coverage on big walls, fewer courses.
- **Shingle/shake (e.g. HardieShingle):** Panels or individual shingles mimicking cedar — gables, accents; more per-square, slower.
- **Trim boards (e.g. HardieTrim):** Fiber cement trim for corners, around openings, bands, fascia — a real material category on a fiber cement job (corners, window/door surrounds, frieze).
- **Finish:** Primed (field-paint after) or factory-finished ColorPlus (pre-colored, touch-up only). ColorPlus changes finish labor (no field painting, but careful handling + touch-up).

## 3.2 Required dimensions for an accurate quote

- **★ Wall area (SF/SQ)** by elevation incl. gables.
- **★ Exposure / product** (lap exposure, panel size) — coverage.
- **★ Openings count/size** — trim surrounds (fiber cement trim) + flashing.

## 3.7 Best-practice notes that change material or labor

1. **Silica dust control is a safety requirement, not a preference.** Cutting fiber cement releases respirable crystalline silica (lung hazard). Use dustless shears, or dust-collecting saws with the proper blade, an outdoor cut station, and respiratory protection. This affects equipment, setup, and labor pace — and it's an OSHA/health obligation. Build it in.
2. **Follow the clearance and flashing rules (manufacturer/warranty).** Fiber cement has required clearances — to grade (~6"), to roofs (~1–2"), to decks/horizontal surfaces, and to other materials — plus kick-out and head flashing. Violating clearances wicks water and voids warranty. These clearances affect layout and starter height. Real detailing.
3. **Blind-nail to framing (or per spec); fasteners corrosion-resistant.** Fasten to studs (16" OC) per the schedule (blind-nail concealed by the next course, or face-nail per product), with hot-dip galv/SS fasteners. Hitting framing matters (not just sheathing) for hold. Spec-driven.
4. **Butt-joint water management.** Plank end joints need flashing behind (or factory joint covers / gapped-and-flashed method) so water at joints drains out, not into the wall. Modern best practice gaps and flashes joints rather than caulking them tight. Affects joint material + method.
5. **ColorPlus vs. primed changes the finish scope hugely.** ColorPlus (factory-finished) = no field painting, just careful handling + edge touch-up — faster overall, premium material cost. Primed = field prime/caulk/2-coat paint, a major added labor + material line. Clarify which; it's a big swing.
6. **Heavy material = 2-person handling + breakage.** Planks/panels are heavy and crack if dropped/mishandled or fastened wrong. Handling labor and a breakage allowance are real. Storage flat and dry before install (it absorbs water).
7. **WRB + drainage behind (rainscreen optional but better).** WRB required; a vented rainscreen (furring) behind fiber cement improves drying and longevity (and is sometimes specified). Affects furring material/labor if speced.
8. **Trim is a substantial line.** Fiber cement (or composite/PVC) trim for corners, window/door surrounds, bands, frieze, and fascia is a real material + labor category — often comparable effort to a meaningful chunk of the field. Estimate trim LF explicitly.
9. **Prime/paint all cut edges (primed product).** Field-cut edges expose the substrate and must be primed/sealed (and painted) or they wick water. A real step on primed product; ColorPlus uses edge touch-up.
10. **Gauge consistent exposure.** Use lap gauges/story pole for uniform exposure and level courses — workmanship that shows. Slightly more setup, better result.
11. **Gables/returns/dormers** — include area + angle-cut waste (and the brittleness = a bit more waste).
12. **Don't install wet / let it acclimate appropriately.** Fiber cement absorbs moisture; install/store dry per spec, and field-paint per the moisture/temperature window. Affects scheduling in wet/cold conditions (relevant here).

Fiber cement (Hardie) re-side, ColorPlus, single-story (illustrative):

# 4. WOOD & ENGINEERED WOOD SIDING

## 4.1 System overview & sub-types

Wood siding is the traditional/natural cladding — real wood (cedar, pine, etc.) or **engineered wood** (e.g. LP SmartSide — wood strands/fibers + resin, more stable and lower-cost than natural, very popular). The estimate is driven by **wall area (field), the trim, openings, fasteners, WRB/flashing — and the finish (wood needs paint/stain, and re-finishing over time).** Natural wood is higher-maintenance and pricier; engineered wood is a strong middle option (real-wood look, easier install than fiber cement, lighter, factory-primed).

**Sub-types:**
- **Engineered wood (LP SmartSide etc.) lap or panel — popular default:** Treated wood-strand product, primed, in lap or panel/B&B. Lighter and easier to cut than fiber cement (no silica), nailable, stable, lower cost. Field-painted (primed) — or some pre-finished. A very common modern choice.
- **Natural cedar/wood lap (bevel/clapboard):** Real cedar/pine bevel siding; classic, premium, higher maintenance (paint/stain, rot risk). Various exposures.
- **Wood shingle/shake (cedar):** Individual cedar shingles/shakes — gables, whole walls, premium, labor-intensive. (Distinct from roofing shakes but related.)
- **Board & batten (wood/engineered):** Vertical boards with battens over joints; furring + vertical layout.
- **Tongue-and-groove / channel / shiplap (wood):** Horizontal or vertical T&G/channel boards; nicer joints, more material/labor.
- **Log/half-log, specialty:** Cabin looks; specialty.

**Finish:** Primed (field paint/stain) vs. pre-finished. Natural wood needs periodic refinishing (maintenance reality to communicate). Back-priming (sealing the back/ends before install) greatly extends wood siding life — a best practice.

## 4.2 Required dimensions for an accurate quote

- **★ Trim scope** — corner boards, surrounds, bands, frieze, fascia (wood/engineered trim).
- **★ Finish: primed (field paint/stain) or pre-finished** — finish labor (big for wood).

## 4.7 Best-practice notes that change material or labor

1. **Back-prime and seal all cut ends (wood/engineered).** Sealing the backs and especially the cut end-grain before install dramatically extends wood siding life (end grain wicks water → rot). A pre-install step (material + labor) that's standard for quality wood work and prevents premature failure. Easy to skip, costly to omit.
2. **Stainless fasteners for cedar/redwood.** Tannins in cedar/redwood corrode regular and even hot-dip galv steel and cause black streaking/staining bleed-through. Use stainless for cedar/redwood. A material spec that affects appearance and longevity.
3. **Finishing is a major separate phase (primed/raw).** Field prime + 2 coats of paint, or stain — this is real labor and material, often rivaling the install. Pre-finished products avoid it (premium cost). Clarify finish; a "primed" quote without painting is incomplete. Natural wood also needs periodic re-finishing — communicate the maintenance reality.
4. **Rainscreen (vented cavity) strongly recommended for wood.** Wood lasts much longer with a vented gap behind (furring) for drying — modern best practice for wood/engineered siding, especially in wet/cold climates. Adds furring material + labor if used; greatly improves longevity. Worth speccing here.
5. **WRB + flashing + clearances.** WRB required; head/kick-out flashing; clearance to grade and roofs (wood rots in contact with wet/soil). Maintain ground clearance (~6–8") and roof clearance. Layout/starter affected.
6. **Engineered wood (LP SmartSide) advantages.** Lighter and easier to cut than fiber cement (no silica), nailable, stable, factory-primed, lower cost than natural cedar — a strong default for the real-wood look without fiber cement's weight/dust or cedar's cost/maintenance. Follow its fastening/clearance spec.
7. **Acclimate and store dry.** Wood/engineered siding should acclimate and be stored flat/dry before install; installing wet wood leads to shrinkage gaps/cupping. Scheduling consideration in wet conditions.
8. **Corner method drives labor.** Corner boards (butt the siding to a trim board — faster, common) vs. mitered corners (cut/fit each course at 45° — slow, refined) vs. woven corners for shingles (very labor-intensive). The chosen corner detail meaningfully changes labor. Capture it.
9. **Knot/cull/grade waste (natural wood).** Natural wood has knots, splits, and grade variation — expect higher waste (cull boards, cut around defects) than engineered/manufactured products. Order more.
10. **Trim is a real line (as fiber cement).** Corner boards, surrounds, bands, frieze, fascia — wood/engineered trim is substantial material + labor. Estimate trim LF explicitly; finish it too.
11. **Nailing for movement/splitting.** Nail per spec (not over-driven, proper placement, pre-drill near ends for natural wood to prevent splitting). Workmanship that prevents splits/cupping.
12. **Gables/returns/dormers** — include area + angle-cut/cull waste.

# APPENDIX — Cross-system siding principles

1. **Siding is surface estimating; the WRB/flashing underneath is the real water control.** Field cladding = wall area ÷ coverage × (1+waste); trim/flashing = openings + perimeter/transition LF. But on every system, **the water-resistive barrier and flashing behind/around the siding are what keep the wall dry** — siding sheds the bulk and drains/breathes; the WRB + flashing manage what gets behind. On a re-side, the WRB and flashing are often the most important (and most valuable) part of the job. Never treat them as afterthoughts or rely on caulk/trim as waterproofing.
2. **Flashing details that prevent rot:** head flashing over openings, **kick-out flashing at roof-wall intersections** (the single most-missed detail — causes major wall rot), proper WRB integration (shingle-style laps over flashing flanges), and material-specific clearances (to grade, roofs, decks). These apply across all four systems.
3. **Include gables, dormers, returns, and angle-cut waste.** The most common takeoff miss is forgetting triangular gable area and the extra cut waste on cut-up elevations. Always measure and add them.
4. **Trim is a real material + labor category** on every system except basic vinyl — corners, opening surrounds, bands, frieze, fascia. Estimate trim LF explicitly; on fiber cement and wood it can rival a meaningful share of the field effort, and it must be finished too.
5. **Finish scope is a major swing on fiber cement and wood.** Pre-finished (ColorPlus / pre-finished wood) vs. field-painted (primed) changes labor enormously. Always clarify; a primed-product quote without the painting line is incomplete.
6. **Fastener compatibility is a material spec:** corrosion-resistant throughout; **stainless for cedar/redwood** (tannins); galv/SS for fiber cement and metal-over-PT; length to reach framing where required. Wrong fasteners stain, streak, or pull out.
7. **One combined difficulty factor, capped (~1.4×), on install labor.** Two-story, cut-up, gables, accents — pick the governing one. Tear-off, WRB/rainscreen, rot repair, field painting, staging, dumpster, dust-control (fiber cement) are **separate line items**, not multipliers.
8. **Re-side: budget a rot/sheathing repair allowance** — invisible until the old siding's off (bottoms of walls, around openings, roof-wall intersections). Communicate it as an allowance, not a fixed number.
9. **Climate detailing for this market:** vented rainscreen aids drying (wood especially); maintain clearances (snow/grade); detail bases below snow-shedding metal roofs; cold/wet affects vinyl brittleness, fiber cement painting windows, and wood acclimation. Real, market-specific.
10. **Match the cutting/safety method to the material:** vinyl (snap-lock tools, brittle in cold), metal (cold-cut only — no abrasive wheels, touch up edges), **fiber cement (silica dust control — shears or dust-collecting saw + respirators, a safety obligation)**, wood (standard saws, SS for cedar, back-prime). Equipment and safety setup differ by system.
